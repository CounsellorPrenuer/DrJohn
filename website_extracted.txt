0500: ADVICE BEFORE STARTING PUBLIC SPEAKING TRAINING
0501: Watch This Video on the Necessity of Public Speaking
0503: Public speaking is one of the most important skills to rise in your career.
0504: MENTAL PREPARATION BEFORE YOU START
0505: FIRST THING – Stop finding ways to escape.
0506: NO HURRY – TAKE TWO WEEKS. Before starting, you must prepare yourself mentally. Take your time.
0507: DECIDE ONCE AND FOR ALL. You cannot avoid learning this skill. Avoiding it can cost your entire career.
0508: Go into a room alone and speak loudly to yourself. Force your mind to believe: YOU CAN DO IT.
0509: You do not need perfect English to be a good speaker.
0510: No one is born a great speaker. All great speakers worked hard.
0511: If you can survive the first two minutes on stage, you will survive the entire speech.
0512: Never get on stage unprepared. Practice, Practice, Practice.
0513: FIRST STEPS BEFORE GOING ON STAGE
0514: STEP 1
0515: Start by telling a bedtime story to a child in your mother tongue. Then tell the same story in English.
0516: STEP 2
0517: Practice in front of friends or family:
0518: First in your mother tongue.
0519: Then in English.
0520: Sit on chairs with your script in hand.
0521: Speak casually to one person at a time.
0522: Pretend to be confident.
0523: You may panic and forget. If that happens:
0524: Take a deep breath.
0525: Smile.
0526: Ask a question to buy time.
0527: Ask someone to summarize what they understood.
0528: Look at your script confidently and continue.
0529: STEP 3
0530: Rewrite your script in your own words.Use short sentences and familiar words. Add a story or experience.
0531: STEP 4
0532: Highlight key words in yellow.Write those key words on a small card and pin it to your script.
0533: STEP 5
0534: Memorize the first 20 lines.
0535: STEP 6
0536: Rehearse at least 7 times in front of a mirror.
0537: STEP 7
0538: Reach the venue 30 minutes early.Mentally adjust to the environment.Carry a spare copy of your opening.
0539: BEST WAYS TO OPEN A SPEECH (First 60 Seconds Are Critical)
0540: Your opening must be clear, catchy, and impactful.
0541: You can start with:
0542: A question
0543: A news incident
0544: A startling statement
0545: A quote
0546: A human-interest story
0547: An elevator pitch
0548: Always tell the audience:
0549: What you are going to speak about
0550: How long you will take
0551: Use facts, analogies, statistics, and opinions (with reasons).
0552: HOW TO START YOUR PRESENTATION
0553: Walk confidently to the podium.
0554: Place your script on it.
0555: Keep your keyword card in your pocket.
0556: Wait for the audience to settle.
0557: Speak the first 3 sentences.
0558: Take a deep breath.
0559: Three Things Will Definitely Happen:
0560: You may forget your script.
0561: Your heart may pound.
0562: You may panic.
0563: What To Do:
0564: Take a deep breath.
0565: Look at your keyword card confidently.
0566: Drink water if needed.
0567: Restart calmly.
0568: If needed, read from your script.
0569: Confidence usually returns within 30 seconds.
0570: DOs & DON’Ts
0571: DOs
0572: Maintain eye contact with one person at a time.
0573: Use short, simple sentences.
0574: Speak clearly and loudly (do not shout).
0575: Face the audience while speaking.
0576: Use your natural accent.
0577: DON’Ts
0578: Do not insult or embarrass anyone.
0579: Do not speak too fast.
0580: Do not read continuously.
0581: Do not apologize unnecessarily.
0582: Do not show relief after finishing.
0583: Do not use very big words to impress.
0584: Avoid filler words, mumbling, and looking down.
0585: Do not exceed your time limit.
0586: SURVIVAL KIT WHEN YOUR MIND GOES BLANK
0587: Take a deep breath and smile.
0588: Look at your notes confidently.
0589: Ask the audience a question.
0590: Share a small story or experience.

0604: KEY POINTS FOR PRESENTATIONS
0605: Choose not more than 5 key points.
0606: Repeat and emphasize your key ideas.
0607: Transition smoothly between points.
0608: Use facts, analogies, and statistics.
0609: Talk instead of reading.
0610: Move naturally and maintain eye contact.
0611: VISUAL AIDS RULES
0612: Use bullet points only (no full sentences).
0613: Follow the 6×6 rule (max 6 lines per slide, 6 words per line).
0614: Use limited colors (max 3).
0615: Ensure good contrast and large fonts.
0616: Do not depend completely on PowerPoint.
0617: Never write “Thank You” on the slide — say it instead.
0618: DRESSING UP
0619: Dress according to the occasion.
0620: When unsure, dress conservatively.
0621: Keep hair neat.
0622: Avoid excessive jewelry.
0623: Maintain proper grooming.
0624: GESTURES
0625: Keep gestures natural.
0626: Avoid hands in pockets.
0627: Avoid exaggerated movements.
0628: Do not clasp hands tightly.
0629: HANDLING QUESTIONS
0630: Let the person ask the question fully.
0631: Rephrase the question.
0632: Repeat it for the audience.
0633: Invite opinions from 2–3 people.
0634: Consolidate answers and give your view.
0635: Ask if the questioner is satisfied.
0636: Thumb Rule:Answer one person, but speak for the whole audience.
0637: CLOSING THE SPEECH
0638: Indicate you are concluding.
0639: Summarize key points.
0640: Invite questions.
0641: End on a positive and friendly note.
0642: Leave the stage calmly.

1795: THE 7S MODEL
1796: Assessing business performance
1797: The 7S model is a framework for assessing the performance of a company. It views all seven elements as equally important because they impact on each other – with failure in one area undermining the others. By appreciating how they are related, and assessing performance from this perspective, companies and teams can better align activities to achieve goals.
1798: Overview
1799: First developed in the 1970s by McKinsey and refined by Tom Peters, Robert Waterman and Richard Pascale, the 7S model works from the principle that success relies on simultaneously pursuing a combination of seven hard and soft aspects of running a business. Known for changing people’s thinking at the time, it still provides a useful framework for assessing and improving a company or how a team is working – identifying gaps and enabling adjustments to be made to ensure that all seven aspects are aligned, working together, and supporting and reinforcing one another. By knowing how things are interrelated, the framework raises awareness of the full impact of any changes.
1800: StrategyThese are plans that determine, define and outline how to fulfill the company’s goals and purpose and to achieve competitive advantage.
1801: StructureThis is how the company is organized and how each part relates to the others.
1802: SystemsThis is about how both formal and informal business processes function.
1803: Shared values (superordinate goals)These are the company’s beliefs, values and guiding mission that draw people together and that directly influence their approach, thinking and actions.
1804: SkillsThese are the capabilities of both the people and the organization.
1805: StaffThis concerns the nature, type and general abilities of the people employed.
1806: StyleThis is the organization’s culture and style of leadership that, along with having an internal impact, determine how people outside the organization view the company.
1807: The main point is that all seven elements are interrelated, with each affecting the others. In this, it can be viewed as an early proponent of holistic business. Significantly – and this is of particular relevance to leaders today – it reveals how underperformance can be attributed to neglect in any one of the seven aspects, regardless of strong focus and capabilities in one or more of the others. Richard Pascale subsequently argued that, while it is generally important to view all seven as equally significant to achieving success, having shared values (superordinate goals) is the element that binds all the others together.
1808: WEEK 21
1809: THE RULE OF 150
1810: A bold way to create the right working conditions
1811: This rule is about limiting the number of people at any one location to 150.
1812: Overview
1813: The rule is based on the idea that 150 is the largest group size that people can deal with – beyond that number, it is increasingly difficult to form bonds with others. If groups are larger, hierarchies, regulations and formal measures are required. However, with fewer than 150, goals can be achieved informally and people work better and are happier, more motivated and more productive.
1814: Why it works
1815: Co-workers find socializing, teamworking, innovating, collaborating and sharing knowledge easier to achieve in groups of fewer than 150 people. By organizing operations into smaller groups, large companies can gain the benefit of smaller groups – being closer, driven, entrepreneurial, supportive and productive.
1816: The rule in practice
1817: Gore Associates, a high-tech firm, uses this rule. It has 15 plants all within 20 kilometres (12 miles) of one another, and each with fewer than 150 employees. It has resisted the option of merging its separate sites – despite potential cost savings – because the small size of each unit ensures that everyone knows everyone else and works well together.
1818: By organizing itself in this way, Gore, despite being a large company with thousands of employees, is still able to enjoy the entrepreneurial approach of a small start-up. Each unit enjoys the benefits of collective management, which are:
1819: • improved communication• greater initiative• flexibility
1820: It is notable that employee turnover is significantly less than the industry average and the company has enjoyed sustained profitability and growth for over 35 years.
1821: This does not mean that Gore has no control or input. It has put a strong managerial system in place to oversee each unit, to ensure that activities are coordinated and efficient. The company also encourages a sense of community and teamwork within these groups – after all, the rule only means that it is possible for workers to form positive bonds with each other, so efforts must still be made to ensure that this happens. In addition, Gore makes sure that it develops a sense of community across the company by encouraging people to communicate and collaborate with workers from other groups.

1823: THE SERVICE PROFIT CHAIN
1824: Managing the vital link between people and profit
1825: The service profit chain highlights how employee engagement drives improvements in company performance. When employees are able to see the impact of their actions, it changes their approach and improves results.
1826: The idea
1827: The service profit chain is based on the premise that market leadership requires an emphasis on managing value drivers — those factors that have the greatest impact on success and provide the most benefit to customers. This concept is then focused on the value drivers that are the most important determinants of success: employee retention, employee satisfaction and employee productivity — it is these that strongly influence customer loyalty, revenue growth and profitability.
1828: How the service profit chain works
1829: In practice: Sears
1830: In the 1990s US-based retailer Sears reversed significant losses by focusing on employee issues in order to turn around the company’s fortunes. They examined:
1831: • how employees felt about working at the company• how employee behaviour affected customers• how customers’ experience affected profits
1832: Sears asked employees to estimate how much profit was made for each dollar sold. The average answer was 46 cents while the real answer was 1 cent — demonstrating that profitability was poorly understood. The company introduced changes in order to engage with employees and to get them to understand what influences profitability — in particular, to make clear the link between employee behaviour, customer satisfaction and company success. By understanding the implications of their actions, it changed their approach, resulting in sustained improvements in profitability.
1833: In practice: B&Q
1834: At UK retailer B&Q, each percentage increase in staff turnover was costing the company £1 million. By reducing staff turnover from 35 to 28 per cent through its Employee Engagement Programme, the company reduced costs and increased turnover per employee by 20 per cent.
1835: WEEK 23
1836: UNDERSTANDING AND AVOIDING INERTIA
1837: When success traps us in the past
1838: It might seem counterintuitive to warn people about the dangers of success but that is exactly what Donald Sull did when he developed the concept of ‘active inertia’ — where people repeat the strategies and activities that have worked well in the past.
1839: A reliance on previous thinking and approaches — the formula of success — can cause a company to fail to respond properly to new developments. By applying past approaches to new conditions, the end result can be a downward spiral — leaving an organization vulnerable to more dynamic companies with approaches better suited to the new environment.
1840: How active inertia works
1841: A firm correctly discerns gradual shifts and developments in the external environment, but fails to respond effectively.
1842: Managers get trapped by success, often responding to the most disruptive changes by accelerating activities that succeeded in the past.
1843: The source of active inertia is a company’s success formula, the unique set of strategic frames, resources, processes, relationships and values that collectively influence managers’ actions.
1844: With time and repetition, people stop considering alternatives to their formula. The individual components of the success formula grow less flexible.
1845: How active inertia happens
1846: Active inertia occurs because people come to rely on a past formula of success, where accepted approaches become entrenched and people stop considering alternatives. Consequently, people continue to respond to external changes by pursuing fixes and activities that worked in the past. However, these responses are likely to be ineffectual because they are based on past success and not current and future needs.
1847: Why a past success formula does not guarantee a successful future
1848: Essentially, like it or not, our brains are lazy — subconsciously preferring the easy route to solving problems and then, equally subconsciously, superimposing a solid layer of reasons to justify our decisions. So it is hardly surprising that our brains fool us into being happy to rely on approaches that have proven successful in the past: it is easy and we have a ready-made wall of rock-solid excuses to hand.
1849: As individuals, our thinking, strategies, methods, use of resources, relationships and values all become firmly entrenched. The consequence for companies is that this formula becomes so deeply embedded that they are left vulnerable when faced with changing conditions.
1850: It is understandable that past approaches should be so revered and relied upon — they are, after all, the reason for the company’s current success. However, we should keep in mind that this formula is exactly that: suited to the current, stable situation — not the future. Companies can suddenly find themselves commercially stranded.
1851: The bottom line is that, when faced with new developments, your approach needs to change accordingly — essentially, the survival of the fittest depends on adaptation.

1854: Business is a total activity
1855: Luis Gallardo’s Six Rs is a total approach to business — where all activities work together, moving the whole company forward in the same direction.
1856: Having all company activities support one other enables us to develop the right mindset, strategy and approach for growing a successful business. This holistic approach ensures that no part of a company undermines overall goals or the activities of another part of the business. The Six Rs are:

1858: Why the Six Rs matter
1859: The Six Rs should work together, supporting one other and never undermining other business activities or goals. As companies can discover to their cost (witness the damage to sales when legal tax avoidance is revealed), any aspect of running a business can have serious consequences. Conversely, when the various corporate activities support one other, they will strengthen the brand and promote success. Essentially, everyone and all activities should pull together. To have parts, even unwittingly, pulling in different directions will derail strategy and cause a company to veer off course.
1860: Reason
1861: The starting point, and ongoing requirement, for setting and directing all activities is to know the reason why you are in business — your vision, values and purpose. This sets the tone and gains commitment and, consequently, has an enormous impact on customers and achieving goals. Your purpose should be communicated to everyone in the organization. Also, by fitting your products and services to your reason and values, customers and employees will understand what your company means.

1863: Managing and maximizing revenues is essential for enacting strategies and building resilience. An often overlooked but critical aspect is the portfolio of clients — it reveals strengths and gaps elsewhere in the company. The important thing is to manage revenues through the prism of the rest of the 6Rs — and to manage the others through the lens of revenue.
1864: Rousers
1865: Engaging your people and aligning their thinking and behaviours to the rest of the company’s activities depend on being able to inspire them. This has an enormous impact on all areas of a business — especially customers — and sets the right conditions for people to be innovative and to adapt successfully to change.
1866: Reputation
1867: Reputation is critical to success. It affects employees as well as current and potential customers and all stakeholders. The important point is that reputation can be affected by any aspect of the business — emphasizing the need to ensure that other activities do not undermine reputation.
1868: Relationships
1869: All business — internal and external — is about handling relationships. Everything is affected, with a direct bearing on profitability, so all relationships should be managed carefully, keeping in mind the importance of the Six Rs approach.
1870: Resilience
1871: Developing resilience enables companies to continue achieving goals, to survive difficult circumstances and to take advantage of opportunities. It enables swift and appropriate responses to any developments and the flexibility to adapt to change. Resilience involves being proactive, prepared and having the right mindset to deal with any events, threats or opportunities.

1874: How to manage your product portfolio
1875: Identifying which products and investments should be continued (and at what level of investment) is a complicated task. Cutting through this confusion, the Boston Consulting Group model (developed by Bruce Henderson) provides a straightforward means of managing your portfolio of products.
1876: How it works
1877: The model uses a matrix, each box representing a type of product: Star, Cash cow, Question mark and Dog. Products are located in a quadrant according to market growth and market share. The category a product falls into enables you to see whether it is worth pursuing. By looking at the matrix, it is easy to see why each category has certain characteristics and prospects.
1878: Star
1879: Given the high market growth, this product is obviously a rising star and should be pursued. Coupled with high market share, the risks are minimal and the return will be high. A note of caution, though, is that a growing market will inevitably cost a lot to keep up with so it is advisable to consider your ability to fund this — especially if there are large set-up costs or if you expect a delay in the product generating revenue.
1880: Cash cow
1881: Clearly, given the large market share, there is still a lot of potential for generating revenue. However, given the low market growth, there may be some limiting factors (such as time or changing technology) that suggest you should milk these products as much as you can before the opportunity for high returns dwindles in a declining market. It would be wise to monitor market conditions closely to prevent losses should the market decline rapidly.
1882: Question mark
1883: If a product falls into this category, there are issues that need to be addressed before a decision can be made. Although there is high market growth, you have to ask yourself whether the low market share will generate enough revenue to justify the investment — especially given the likely high costs of keeping pace with a growing market. A key factor in making a decision is having deep-enough pockets either to wait for higher returns as the market grows or to turn it into a Star by securing a stronger market share.
1884: Dog
1885: With low market share and low market growth, this product is going nowhere fast. Clearly, it is not worth pursuing. Sometimes, you may wish to continue with this type of product if it provides other benefits — such as maintaining customer loyalty for your overall brand.

1887: THE PARETO PRINCIPLE
1888: Finding the right locus and answer using the 80:20 rule
1889: Pareto analysis arose from Vilfredo Pareto’s observation that many activities break down into an 80:20 ratio, where 80 per cent of output is due to 20 per cent of the contributory factors. This observation is now used to focus business strategy, problem-solving and operations on the key inputs that are responsible for 80 per cent of the outcome.
1890: How it works
1891: The 80:20 ratio applies both to positive and negative situations, providing a useful means of dealing quickly with problems or opportunities. In other words, by identifying the small number of key factors that are contributing most to a situation, we can better focus efforts to achieve the desired result.
1892: Pareto analysis is only as good as the data that is used, so we need to ensure that all contributory factors are identified and that appropriate and revealing parameters and measures are established and interpreted correctly. Although not everything falls neatly into an 80:20 rule, Pareto analysis is still useful for identifying the main causal factors.
1893: This simple example shows how the process works:
1894: Research and discuss the issue, identifying all contributory factors.
1895: Decide an appropriate time period and method of measurement.
1896: Measure how frequently each factor occurs (or another measure, such as cost).
1897: Rank the factors in descending order, with the largest one first.
1898: Calculate the frequency of each factor as a percentage of the total occurrences (or cost).
1899: Calculate the cumulative percentage (current percentage plus all previous percentages).
1900: Depict this information on a graph – with ‘frequency as a percentage of total’ as a bar chart and ‘cumulative percentage’ as a line, adding a third line showing the 80 per cent cut-off point.
1901: All factors that appear to the left of the intersection of the two lines are the ones contributing to 80 per cent of the result — these are the factors to focus on.

1904: BLUE OCEAN STRATEGY
1905: Creating unique market opportunities
1906: A Blue Ocean Strategy is one where the key to success lies not in competing directly with rivals within a market, but in creating an entirely new market where there are currently no competitors and where the potential for high returns is vast.
1907: Developed by W. Chan Kim and Renee Mauborgne, Blue Ocean Strategy involves a change in strategic thinking towards a mindset that challenges existing market boundaries, rewrites the rules of competition, and creates a new, as yet uncontested, market space. The theory outlines two attitudes to competition: Red Oceans and Blue Oceans.
1908: The current marketplace for all products and services is made up of Red Oceans (bloody battlegrounds), where boundaries are clearly defined and companies operate within the boundaries of their accepted Red Ocean markets. Here, the entrenched battleground is one where companies compete to gain extra market share within the current market boundary.
1909: A very different attitude pervades the Blue Oceans. These are areas of deep, uncharted, almost limitless potential where the aim is not to compete on traditional grounds but to develop products and services that create entirely new markets. In essence, it is creating customers that do not yet exist.
1910: At its core, Blue Ocean Strategy believes that it is better to create tomorrow’s customers through developing a new market rather than scrabbling around trying to capture existing customers in the current marketplace. There may be many justifications for this approach but, quite simply, the reason seems straightforward: to create a monopoly situation and reap the high rewards before competitors enter the new market.
1911: Value creation
1912: Value is achieved by integrating the utility of the product with its cost and price. It is not a case of choosing between competing through managing costs or product differentiation: it is about pursuing both. It is this that creates the value that appeals across customer groups, drawing them into a new market. Think of this as maximizing the gap between the utility of the product and its price (facilitated by lower costs) — the larger this gap, the higher the value and the more it attracts customers.
1913: Blue Ocean Strategy relies on four main principles:
1914: Challenging existing market boundaries.Reconstruct the marketplace, identifying and creating new markets and customers. The Blue Ocean is a vast place where demand is unrealized — it doesn’t yet exist. The aim is to bring this demand into existence.
1915: Keeping focused on the overall picture.Be clear about your goals: what matters and needs to be achieved.
1916: Minimizing risk.Assess current industry standards and decide what can be:a. eliminated — things that are not necessaryb. reduced — things that do not need to be done to a high standardc. raised — things that should be done betterd. created — things that have never been offered before.
1917: Planning careful implementation.You will need to overcome barriers and secure the resources and the support of your people (especially key influencers).

1927: CompetitiveUsing competitors’ standards to set targets that match or improve upon their performance
1928: IndustrySetting benchmarks that are industry standards
1929: Best-in-classTo match or surpass the standards of the best companies in any industry or country

1931: The data should be free from bias or vested interests. Using an external company to gather evidence and measure standards will help to maintain impartiality.

This ensures that benchmarking is focused on the issues that matter now rather than reflecting the past, and is not blinkered by a narrow, internal focus that risks delivering more of the same.

1937: THE PRODUCT LIFE CYCLE
1938: Managing your product portfolio
1939: From development and launch, through its peak to eventual decline, a product’s life cycle determines the strategy needed to optimize its return at each stage and to develop further products to ensure ongoing profitability and competitiveness.
1940: Although not an exact science, the duration of each stage varies according to the product and the markets involved. Some life cycles are obviously shorter than others — such as technology products. With very short life cycles, it is essential to maximize returns as quickly as possible and to be continually developing the next products. A long-lasting branded product, despite undergoing many life cycles, enjoys continuity from its brand name. Companies, however, still have to manage the life cycles of such branded products — planning the next improvement and managing the replacement of the current version.
1941: There are five stages in the product life cycle:
1942: Development — this includes entirely new products and changes or improvements to existing products
1943: Introduction — at this stage, costs can be high relative to revenue
1944: Growth — revenue rises and offsets costs
1945: Maturity — growth slows and competition rises
1946: Decline — sales decline due to increased competition or changing customer preferences
1947: The following describes tactics appropriate to each stage:
1948: Development
1949: Development can be very costly, with unexpected delays, so cash-flow issues are paramount. Researching what customers are looking for and testing prototypes with potential customers will help you develop the right products with fewer glitches — as well as promoting a ready-made pool of customers. Importantly, product development is an ongoing process, ensuring that new products or improvements to existing products are ready to replace current products.
1950: Introduction
1951: Getting the launch right is essential. Raising product awareness quickly requires promotional and advertising investment — depending on the nature of the product, targeting early adopters can be useful at this stage. An aggressive pricing strategy can achieve fast market penetration — although this will depend on the brand’s attributes. You could also consider minimizing distribution costs by limiting the availability of the product.
1952: Growth
1953: In the face of more competition, but still with considerable potential revenue and falling unit costs, strategy needs to focus on outcompeting rivals, delivering extra value to customers and increasing market share. Further promotional offers, marketing and advertising campaigns, attractive prices and promoting the product’s brand will strengthen your position.
1954: Maturity
1955: Given the influx of competitors, a company is faced with several strategic options to strengthen its market share, including: product differentiation, entering new markets, attracting rivals’ customers, a price war, and reducing costs to maintain competitive pricing and profitability. It is important at this stage to monitor the financial situation and the viability of the different options.
1956: Decline
1957: With falling sales and reduced margins, any plans and further investment should be considered carefully. Reducing the available options for the product and reducing the number of markets the product is offered in will reduce costs. Catering to your core customers to cement their loyalty can also boost profits at this stage. Other tactics to extend the life of a product include product extensions and entering previously untapped markets.

1960: SYSTEMS THINKING
1961: Building better companies
1962: A company is a collection of systems, and systems within systems. These all need to operate individually and collectively, to drive the business forward. A company’s systems need to work with strategy, and they need to be open, adaptive and understood.
1963: Traditional approaches to strategy have emphasized the mechanics of how things work. This can result in too much complexity and ‘over-engineering’, with processes and systems being overly focused on the present, unable to adapt and failing to win people over. The fundamental flaw is setting a predetermined solution at the start of any redesign, which then influences subsequent thinking, narrowing views and ambitions, and misses better options. Often, the result of re-engineering is an expensive disappointment.
1964: In The Fifth Discipline, Peter Senge revolutionized business re-engineering by arguing that solutions should be considered only after fully understanding the relationships within and between systems (including the behaviours involved) and examining all related problems and issues. Essentially: go back to basics, look deeper and search further, before you start thinking about solutions. Such open systems thinking builds teams, promotes creativity and develops new approaches. It works with the company’s long-term strategy, enabling adaptability and continual improvement. It is not the easiest approach: it is time-consuming and mentally demanding and generates an overwhelming number of questions. It works best when the right culture and mindset exist.
1965: There are seven steps to successful systems thinking:
1966: Explore the situationGather the information you need without making judgments or looking for causes and effects. At this stage, do two things:• Cast your net wide, collating as much information as possible.• Be objective and detached (see things as they are, without an agenda).
1967: Describe the systemTo understand what you are dealing with, list and describe the things that have happened — including the culture, people and atmosphere. Identify, date and examine trends and patterns. Position each factor on a diagram to show the relationships that exist between them. This highlights how aspects work together and reveals negative and positive feedback loops to enable you to analyze the systems in more depth later.
1968: Build modelsMathematical and IT tools are useful but they will take you only so far because systems need to be considered as they really function if they are to be understood and improved.
1969: Compare your model to what is actually happeningCheck your model against reality to see whether it fits and whether you have understood it correctly or have missed something.
1970: Identify potential improvementsOnce you have confirmed that your model is an accurate representation of what is happening, explore ways in which the system can be improved.
1971: Implement your improvementsMonitor changes and identify any further improvements that could be made. It is essential to win people over — successful change depends on people’s willingness to work positively with the new systems.
1972: Repeat the processSystems thinking is a continuous activity; companies need to adapt to change and to take advantage of new opportunities.

1974: MARKET BARRIERS
1975: Protecting your profits
1976: Market exit and entry barriers have both positive and negative effects on profit, depending on your company’s position and on the impact the barriers have on your competitors. A key aspect of awareness of market barriers is that they increase our focus on external issues. In short, it forces us to look up and see the business horizon in much greater detail.
1977: Overview
1978: The word ‘barrier’ is slightly misleading. While barriers will certainly make you do your sums, consider the ramifications and prepare contingency plans, they also deter your competitors. And that is the point: use barriers to your advantage. Your strategy must include careful calculations about the costs involved and you must balance these against the revenue and market dominance potential, but it should also look for how to exploit barriers to your advantage.
1979: The matrix below summarizes the impact of barriers to entry and exit on profitability.
1980: Low entry barriers – Returns: stable | Profit: lowLow entry barriers – Returns: at risk | Profit: lowHigh entry barriers – Returns: stable | Profit: highHigh entry barriers – Returns: at risk | Profit: high

1982: Entry barriers
1983: There are many barriers to entry, including:• the high cost of capital• other companies owning patents and proprietary technology• high research and development costs of developing necessary products• expensive technology• existing companies enjoying economies of scale that you can’t afford to match• a restricted number of government licences• the expense of (or lack of access to) effective distribution channels• your product not being different enough from market leaders.
1984: Exit barriers
1985: There are many exit barriers, including:• high fixed costs• few buyers for your expensive, specialized equipment• contractual salary, redundancy and pension commitments• legal regulations• outstanding contractual obligations• being tied to other companies• risk to brand image.
1986: Not only do you need to understand all the costs, legalities and brand issues, you need to understand how barriers work: how they affect you and, importantly, how they will affect your current and potential competitors. Do this and you will determine the business strategy that is right for your company.
1987: For example, the ideal scenario for an established company is to have high entry barriers and low exit barriers. The reasons are self-evident: high entry barriers deter others from entering the market you are already operating in; low exit barriers will not cause you a problem should you decide to change course.
1988: A much less favourable scenario is having low entry barriers but high exit barriers. Obviously, with low entry barriers, competitors can flood into the market. Unfortunately, the high exit barriers will make it difficult and expensive to leave the market, restricting your strategic options in the future.

2005: PORTER’S GENERIC COMPETITIVE STRATEGIES
2006: Choosing the road ahead
2007: Porter’s Generic Competitive Strategies describe how a company develops competitive advantage across its chosen market. There are three generic strategies: cost leadership, differentiation and focus.
2008: Overview
2009: A company chooses to pursue one of two types of competitive advantage: either with lower costs than its competitors, or by differentiating itself along dimensions valued by customers so it can command a higher price. A company also chooses one of two types of scope: either focus (offering its products to selected segments of the market) or industry-wide, offering its product across many market segments. The generic strategy reflects choices made about both the type of competitive advantage and the scope. The concept was first described by Michael Porter in 1980.
2010: Cost leadership
2011: The strategic aim is to offer competitive prices by reducing costs and to also use lower costs to raise profit margins, fund discount campaigns, or launch an aggressive price war to gain market share and eliminate the competition. Reducing costs can also open up new markets that were less able to sustain higher prices. Another advantage of lowering costs is providing flexibility should suppliers raise prices unexpectedly and suddenly, without you also having to raise prices.
2012: The risks, however, are that other companies can copy your methods, eroding any advantage you have, and the lack of investment in research and development will leave your products looking dated and inefficient compared to those of competitors with better equipment and methods.
2013: Differentiation
2014: Developing distinctive products for different segments separates you from the competition. It creates product desirability, strengthens your brand, promotes customer loyalty, provides competitive advantage, enables higher prices and delivers higher returns. Your products can be differentiated from those of your competitors but you can also differentiate your own products from one another to target different customer groups and markets.
2015: The risks are higher costs and waste and the potential for more complex operations.
2016: Focus
2017: While focus incorporates aspects of cost leadership and differentiation, it is concerned with targeting products and services at one market segment, gaining increased share in that segment. The risk is that this will produce a narrow view that is overly focused on the short term, on too few factors, and on a less lucrative or unstable market and thus fails to see potential elsewhere.

2072: RESOURCES AND THE CRITICAL PATH
2073: The drivers of business performance
2074: ‘Resources’ is an overused term in business but any factor providing value or benefit, from whatever origin, is a resource that can be used to benefit the business. Increasing and strengthening resources over time can be seen as the critical path to business success.
2075: Managing resources
2076: Assessing which resources are important involves taking a view across the whole of the business and identifying those factors, direct or indirect, tangible or intangible, that can be expanded and used for competitive advantage. Understanding which resources are most important and how they should be managed requires a clear understanding of the nature of each resource, in terms of the following:
2077: • The interaction between resources. Resources can combine in a cycle to accelerate their growth. For example, rising sales volumes may lead to more cash and more internal capacity, both of which can be used to generate increasing sales, perhaps by entering new markets, in a self-sustaining cycle. Similarly, product quality (an intangible resource) may lead to increased sales, and this in turn can generate sufficient cash to continue improving product quality (and continue increasing sales). In the same way that resources can interact to reinforce one another, they can also interact by limiting one another.
2078: • The fragility of the resource. Cash, quality, customers, staff, reputation and most other resources can all disappear with remarkable speed and ease. It is, therefore, important to control the main factors likely to damage or undermine resources. For example: cash needs to be monitored and controlled; quality can be eroded by suppliers; service can be undermined by the attitudes of personnel; and brand reputation may be damaged by the actions of distributors.
2079: • The quality of resources. It is worth considering how the quality of resources can be developed. For example, a customer base is a valuable resource, but its quality might be improved by increasing customer loyalty to your brand – for instance, by using customer loyalty schemes.
2080: How resources affect performance
2081: Resources have a special characteristic: they fill and drain over time. Since a firm’s performance at any time directly reflects the resources available, it is essential that we understand how those resources develop over time and how we can control that process. To build strong business performance, we need to know:
2082: • how many resources are available• how fast these numbers are changing• how strongly these factors are being influenced by things under our control and by other forces• how resources interrelate with one another
2083: In a system where resources are integrated and working together, what matters is not the uniqueness of individual resources but how they combine and work together to deliver value for customers. To manage resources and ensure that they drive performance in the desired direction, start by understanding how resources work together.
2084: WEEK 38
2085: DEVELOPING INTANGIBLE RESOURCES
2086: Intangibles: what they are, why they matter, and what they can do for you
2087: Soft ‘intangible’ factors can play a crucial role in developing a business’s competitive performance. For example, a charity with strong commitment from its donors will achieve its goals more easily, and a business with a culture that encourages coaching, risk-taking, new ideas and avoids blame is more likely to make improvements and achieve progress.
2088: Unfortunately, intangibles can be tough to manage. You may easily borrow cash, buy production capacity or hire staff, but it is slow and difficult to build staff morale, a strong reputation, support from a charity’s donors or to generate new ideas.
2089: Overview
2090: Resources can typically be classified into two of four categories: either direct or indirect and tangible or intangible.
2091: • Direct resources are those factors such as staff expertise, cash or intellectual property that can be developed and nurtured by the business. Customers are, perhaps, the biggest single direct resource. (Viewing customers as a resource focuses thinking on how to accumulate and retain them.)
2092: • Indirect resources are those factors that have a bearing on the quality, strength and value of resources. For example, effective training and development policies are an indirect resource, as they build the effectiveness of staff expertise.
2093: • Tangible resources are those that can be physically seen, such as cash, inventory, sales volumes and customers; typically, these have the highest profile within the organization, as they are the most apparent.
2094: • Intangible resources such as service quality, brand reputation or staff expertise are also vitally important to success.
2095: Of these, intangible resources can be the hardest to manage (and the easiest to ignore). Several techniques will help ensure that intangible resources are working well with the rest of the business:
2096: • Identify the most important intangibles. Since your performance relies on concrete resources, assess whether an intangible factor is likely to influence your ability to win or lose the resources. It is not advisable to waste time examining too many factors, as it is more likely that only one or two factors will have a significant impact.
2097: • Be clear which of these factors genuinely ‘accumulate’ through time and which are simply current features of the business. ‘Quality’ and ‘service’ reflect the balance between what has to be done and what is available to do it, in which case they do not accumulate. Reputation, motivation, commitment and relationships, on the other hand, are built up and drain away over time in response to events.
2098: • Assess intangibles carefully, identify the best measure and also the events causing each intangible to rise or fall. Look for ways to strengthen intangibles.
2099: • Build intangible measures into your performance tracking system. Reporting systems now commonly incorporate soft measures (as distinct from hard data, such as financial measures) from various parts of the organization, recognizing that soft measures such as engagement or reputation are crucial to a well-performing system.
2100: • If you don’t know, don’t ignore the issue. Soft factors are influencing your organization, continually and powerfully. Remember, if you choose to ignore them, you are not, in fact, really leaving them out. Instead, you are assuming that they are satisfactory and unchanging. This is unlikely to remain correct, so make your best estimate and start tracking and understanding them.
2101: WEEK 39
2102: MARKET POSITIONING AND VALUE CURVES
2103: Choosing the best position in the market for your business or product
2104: A value curve is a way of highlighting customers’ needs and preferences. This can be used to understand a firm’s competitive position, as well as potential trade-offs, opportunities and areas for further development.
2105: Competing firms emphasize and trade off different things that customers value. For example:
2106: • The UK retailer The Body Shop traded the slick packaging, clinical approach and glamorous image traditionally favored by the cosmetics industry in return for a lower price and a more sustainable identity.• In the USA South-West Airlines pioneered low-cost aviation by trading the features of traditional air travel in return for the benefits of cheap, point-to-point travel.• Multiplex cinemas traded the conventional convenience and centrality of town centre locations in return for the benefits of space and a different experience for customers.• Home Depot expanded into out-of-town locations on freeways and employed ex-contractors as a way of providing a new level of service and value for customers who did not typically visit home building stores.
2107: The concept of value curves highlights several points about market positioning:
2108: • Competing firms emphasize and trade off different values (e.g. luxury may be traded for a lower price).• Customers value specific features (e.g. price, packaging) differently at different times.• Different values enable firms to target new, different – and possibly unfulfilled – market segments, potentially increasing the size of the market.• Initially, strategic innovators (e.g. South-West Airlines) create new ‘market space’, gradually redefining the market.• It can be extremely difficult, if not impossible, for incumbents to successfully copy new arrivals. This is because internal cultural and resource issues keep firms anchored in their conventional way of working.• When reviewing a value curve, consider the trend: how are things changing?
2109: WEEK 40
2110: COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS: PORTER’S FIVE FORCES
2111: How competitive is your company?
2112: Porter’s Five Forces model provides a deeper understanding of a firm’s current competitiveness and highlights options to improve competitiveness.
2113: Michael Porter outlines five forces for competitive analysis:
2114: New entrants
2115: Substitute products
2116: Buyers
2117: Suppliers
2118: Existing competitors
2119: 1. New entrants
2120: Ask yourself how easy it is for new companies to enter the market. There are many factors to consider, including barriers to entry (such as patents and high set-up costs), attractiveness of profit margins and the strength of your brand.
2121: 2. Substitutes
2122: Assess how easy it is for your products to be substituted by other products. This includes all alternatives – not just similar products. For example, airlines compete with train and coach companies, not just other airlines.
2123: 3. Buyers
2124: Review how strong your buyers are. Is it a buyers’ market? Can buyers switch to competitors easily? Are some of your customers in such a strong position that this leaves you vulnerable? If your business-to-business buyers are operating at low profit margins, what impact will this have on your company?
2125: 4. Suppliers
2126: Assess the strength of your suppliers. Are you dependent on a particular supplier – and how can this be mitigated? Does the supplier rely on your custom or could it easily take its operating capacity to other companies or sell directly to your customers? Could you use alternative products or methods to reduce your vulnerability?
2127: 5. Existing competitors
2128: Understand your competitors and how you compare to them.
2129: • What threat do they pose?• What are their strengths and weaknesses?• Could there be a price war or other aggressive strategies – and would you be able to survive such tactics?• Are they innovative?• Are customers able to move to other companies easily?• How many competitors are there?• Which companies are the strongest?• Are there any newcomers ready to take the market by storm or render your products redundant?
2130: Assessing competitiveness through all five forces will help you to determine how the company is performing, its strengths and weaknesses and the direction it is heading in. Because a weakness of Porter’s approach is the focus on external issues, it is often used alongside complementary models that are better at revealing the internal issues that impact on a company’s competitiveness.
2131: WEEK 41
2132: INNOVATION HOTSPOTS
2133: How to build a culture of innovation
2134: Developed by Professor Lynda Gratton, Innovation Hotspots occur where conditions are right and there is encouragement – they cannot be formally imposed. Encouragement is needed in four areas, which are:
2135: a co-operative mindset
2136: boundary spanning
2137: developing a sense of purpose
2138: productive capacity
2139: 1. A co-operative mindset
2140: A co-operative mindset results from a company’s practices, processes, behaviours and norms – the behaviour of top management is significant. People have to want to share both explicit and tacit knowledge. Several elements are vital:
2141: • Consider relationships when selecting staff.• Emphasize relationships in inductions.• Provide mentoring.• Emphasize collective rewards over individual ones.• Establish structures that facilitate peer-to-peer working.• Develop social responsibility.
2142: 2. Boundary spanning
2143: This involves thinking beyond your immediate boundaries – seeing the larger picture. This involves:
2144: • being undeterred by physical distance• welcoming a diverse range of ideas, insights, experience and people• being willing and able to explore issues together• networking and building bridges for others to cross• using different levels of co-operation (e.g. use strong ties where developing trust quickly is important; use weak ties to generate a lot of ideas)• listening and reflecting in conversations rather than just pushing a point of view
2145: 3. Developing a sense of purpose
2146: Pose challenging (or ‘igniting’) questions. These don’t have a ‘right’ answer; they invite exploration of options. They inspire and engage people and lead to a new vision that provides purpose and energy.
2147: 4. Productive capacity
2148: Ensuring that a hotspot realizes its full potential relies on building productive capacity by:
2149: • understanding and appreciating the talents of others• obtaining practical, public and explicit commitment from participants• harnessing the creative energy which results from problem-solving and decision-making• synchronizing time, especially where different time zones have to be accommodated or where there are different attitudes to time• ensuring that pressure is neither too high, where people burn out, or too low, where they lose interest
2150: Innovation relies on teamwork, agility and the ability to lead change. Crucially, it is about mindset: you need to think like an innovator and you need to encourage this in others. Innovation isn’t only about products – it’s about understanding customers and building a brand, improving efficiency, reducing costs, improving the quality and quantity of people’s work and removing constraints.
2151: WEEK 42
2152: DEEP DIVE PROTOTYPING
2153: Developing creative, practical solutions
2154: Developed and popularized by the consultancy firm IDEO, Deep Dive Prototyping is a focused, team-based approach to generating solutions to a particular problem or challenge. It is a useful way of stimulating creative thinking and to capture and fine-tune ideas.
2155: The process
2156: A deep dive combines brainstorming and prototyping (building and exploring a potential solution) to devise actions that will help move a business forward. There is no time limit, and the main stages are:
2157: • Build a team that has a mix of strengths and approaches.• Define the design challenge – to do this, understand your market, customers, technology and constraints and use this information to develop key themes.• Visit experts, and gather information on markets, customers – and ideas generally.• Share ideas.• Brainstorm and vote – this involves intensive brainstorming and discussion to imagine new concepts and ideas based around the main themes.• Develop a fast prototype.• Test and refine the prototype, streamlining ideas to improve the prototype and to overcome obstacles – at this stage, evaluate and prioritize ideas and decide how they can be implemented.• Focus on the prototype and produce a final solution.• Give credit to those involved – this promotes motivation and encourages continued innovative thinking.
2158: WEEK 43
2159: DEVELOPING CREATIVE THINKING
2160: Making creativity the norm
2161: Edward de Bono sees creativity as a learnable skill, one that is best harnessed through formal techniques. He proposes that parallel thinking is a more useful and effective means of putting creative talent to work.
2162: Formal creativity works because it works with the way everyone’s brains work: both consciously and subconsciously, we automatically filter, categorize, process and organize information. Building on this, de Bono argues that parallel thinking is more effective for generating the results that make a difference to companies. (Parallel thinking is when each individual puts forward their own thoughts in parallel with those of others. In this way, each individual is able to complement, enrich and build on one another’s thinking, rather than competing or attacking the thoughts of others.)
2163: The reason why this is more important than ever is because what companies previously relied on for competitive advantage – competence, information and technology – are now easy-to-obtain commodities. These are all buyable commodities, enabling your competitors to rapidly erode any advantage you may have had. Today, what matters is creating value from these commodities.
2164: Understanding creativity
2165: Creativity solves problems, challenges existing methods, and provides a better and constantly improving way forward. Given the reward, companies need to know how best to harness creativity in a way that is useful. A major flaw in traditional brainstorming is that it assumes that, if you give people the freedom to express themselves, they will magically become creative. This is not the case. For organizations, useful creativity needs to be a formal activity that requires thinking that provokes and challenges a current situation and then searches for answers.
2166: Provoke, challenge and search for solutions
2167: Given the brain’s natural inclination to organize information and think laterally, we can tackle issues by simply taking a random starting point. Our brains will automatically process information, make connections and point us in new directions. Allowing such randomness in selecting a starting point is important. It suggests new possibilities and takes thinking along new paths. Significantly, it is likely that our brains have already processed information and are subconsciously suggesting such opening gambits because they could be highly relevant. This serves to break us out of the current doldrums and set us on a new course.
2168: Next, our new thinking needs to move forward: to challenge the information it is processing. Just because something has always been done a certain way does not mean it is carved in stone: methods can always be improved upon. Constantly questioning and challenging is a mindset that is a huge source of competitive advantage precisely because it is the way that companies create value from their resources. An important point to remember is that even when something seems to be working and is successful it doesn’t mean it is the best that it can be. Once thinking challenges the norm, we will automatically explore alternative and potentially better solutions.
2169: Creating a culture of creativity in a world where competence, knowledge and technology are no longer enough is now the true source of success.
2170: WEEK 44
2171: THE DISCOVERY CYCLE (ORCA)
2172: Evaluating innovations
2173: Discovery – making things known or visible – is a vital precursor for innovation. The Discovery Cycle is a way of choosing new ideas that are profitable and scalable.
2174: The Discovery Cycle has four stages, summarized in the acronym ORCA:
2175: Observation. Understand how the world is changing – for example, by looking for anomalies, paradoxes, peripheral developments and direct experience.
2176: Reflection. Techniques that work best at this stage include zooming in and out, using a muse, suspending judgement, slowing down, reflecting on what’s missing, restructuring data to simplify patterns, juxtaposing pieces of different information (bisociation) and taking time to rest.
2177: Conversation. People set the pace and scope for innovation, so the best techniques to use at this stage include contrasting views, setting the agenda, framing the issues and generating hypotheses.
2178: Analysis. The final stage of the Discovery Cycle involves gathering systematic evidence, classifying and categorizing data, naming, completing data analysis and hypothesizing.
2179: Lessons from great innovators
2180: What lessons do innovators have for us? Several come to mind:
2181: • Build on the ideas of others / collaborate. That should be easy for scientists who are, in the words of Isaac Newton, ‘standing on the shoulders of giants’.• Take an unorthodox, distinctive approach.• Embrace diversity.• Create a diverse, open and creative culture.• Develop empathy for the consumer or customer (understand people).• Execute and practically take action.• Be confident and bold.• Find your motivation; enjoy your work.
2182: This list also highlights three other vital points:
2183: Innovation relies on teamwork, agility and the ability to lead change, the other elements of this programme.
2184: Innovation is about mindset: you need to think like an innovator and you need to encourage that in others.
2185: Innovation isn’t only about products: it is about improving efficiency, reducing costs, improving the quality and quantity of people’s work, removing constraints – and that’s just internally; it also means serving and understanding customers, building a brand – and more.
2186: WEEK 45
2187: THE FORTUNE AT THE BOTTOM OF THE PYRAMID (BOP)
2188: Developing the innovator’s mindset
2189: If a company goes to the bottom of the wealth pyramid and builds affordable products, creates awareness and provides access, then the market is phenomenal.
2190: The late Professor C.K. Prahalad argued that there is a ‘poverty penalty’ where the poorest people pay more for everything because they don’t have a choice: they are stuck with local monopolies and bad products and services.
2191: Research recently highlighted by the World Resources Institute shows that the world’s four billion poorest people represent a US$5 trillion market opportunity. There are several other issues at the bottom of the pyramid:
2192: • Pricing is vital. At the BOP, you need to start with an affordable price, understanding that price minus profit equals the acceptable level of cost. This different way of thinking leads to a new range of exciting options.• Innovation is essential. This can be accelerated and improved by focusing on BOP markets because minor, incremental changes won’t be enough: the market requires a fundamental rethink.• Businesses need to substitute investment for collaboration. Management time is needed to increase collaboration – and it is cheaper than simply investing cash.
2193: Companies that ignore growth markets will be left behind – and will have five years, at best, before businesses from growth markets start competing with them.
2194: Developing the innovator’s mindset
2195: Where can you improve your approach to innovation? Ask yourself the following questions and mark yourself out of 10 for each attribute: this will help highlight areas for improvement.
2196: When innovating, how effectively do you:
2197: • engage as many people as possible …?• … and build an open, diverse and positive team?• define the specific challenge or issue?• challenge assumptions: yours and other people’s?• confront challenges and problems?• understand that good ideas can come from anywhere?• follow through – by being practical and realistic, and planning implementation?• focus on the benefits as well as the potential pitfalls?• question? Questioning is a great way both to provide support (e.g. what help do you need?) and challenge (how can we do this faster/cheaper?)• give praise and credit: build momentum (revolutions fail, flywheels succeed)?• be open, build relationships?• remove constraints, tirelessly?• remember the essentials of leading change? (See Number 35.)• balance intuition and analysis?• build collaboration and teamwork? (Think of the 5Ms: meaning, mindset, measurement, mobilizing, mechanisms for renewal.)• avoid the pitfalls of decision-making? (See the description of inhibitors below – which ones are your greatest vulnerability?)• consciously develop your skills?• design matters? (This affects how people feel about something: whether it’s credible, engaging, worthwhile.)
2199: WEEK 46
2200: THE SIX THINKING HATS
2201: If you want to get ahead, get a hat
2202: Created by Edward de Bono, the Six Thinking Hats technique explains the different styles of thinking we use while making decisions.
2203: Overview
2204: People usually have a preferred thinking style. While this style may be useful, it can limit thinking and cause us to miss better solutions. The Six Thinking Hats method allows us to deliberately switch thinking styles so that problems are viewed from multiple perspectives. This leads to better, more balanced decisions.
2205: Using only one approach, such as logical thinking, may ignore emotional reactions, risks, or creative possibilities. Applying multiple hats gives a fuller picture and improves both decision-making and implementation.
2206: The Six Thinking Hats
2207: White HatFocuses on facts, data, and information.Looks at what information is available and what is missing.Analyzes trends, evidence, and objective data.
2208: Red HatUses intuition, emotions, and gut feelings.Considers how people may emotionally react to a decision.Allows expression of feelings without justification.
2209: Black HatLooks at risks, problems, and weaknesses.Asks why an idea might fail.Helps identify dangers and prepare contingency plans.
2210: Yellow HatFocuses on optimism and benefits.Looks at value, advantages, and positive outcomes.Helps maintain motivation during challenges.
2211: Green HatEncourages creativity and new ideas.Explores alternatives and innovative solutions.Free-flowing thinking with minimal criticism.
2212: Blue HatFocuses on control, structure, and process.Commonly used by facilitators or leaders.Manages discussion flow and ensures productive thinking.

2214: INNOVATION CULTURE
2215: Peter Drucker’s seven steps for developing a creative culture
2216: Innovation is not limited to a few individuals or departments. It is a company-wide activity that requires the right mindset, culture, and systems. Profitable ideas can come from anyone, anywhere, at any time.
2217: Where does innovation come from?
2218: Innovation emerges when people are encouraged to question, think critically, and share ideas openly. It is not only about big breakthroughs; small, incremental improvements also create strong competitive advantages. Innovation does not depend solely on large research budgets. It is embedded in everyday thinking, operations, and decision-making.
2219: Creating an innovative organization
2220: Innovative organizations differ because they actively support creativity. They encourage questioning, challenge assumptions, and adapt systems to support innovation. They also collaborate with external experts to strengthen internal capabilities.
2221: Peter Drucker’s seven steps to promote innovation
2222: Analyze unexpected successes and understand why they worked.
2223: Examine situations where outcomes differed from expectations.
2224: Challenge accepted underperformance and question why it is tolerated.
2225: Identify and take advantage of changes in the market.
2226: Observe social changes to uncover new opportunities.
2227: Monitor economic changes and recognize business possibilities.
2228: Understand how new ideas, information, and technology affect customers.
2229: Five catalysts for successful innovation
2230: ConsciousnessPeople understand organizational goals and believe they can contribute.
2231: MultiplicityTeams include diverse skills, backgrounds, experiences, and ideas.
2232: ConnectivityStrong, trusting relationships are encouraged across teams and functions.
2233: AccessibilityPeople have access to resources, time, leaders, and decision-makers.
2234: ConsistencyInnovation is embedded in leadership style, systems, and daily practices.
2235: To build a strong innovation culture, reflect on which of these catalysts need improvement and identify clear actions to strengthen them.
2236: WEEK 48
2237: DISNEY’S CREATIVITY STRATEGY
2238: When you need more than just the bare necessities
2239: We all have a preferred thinking style – some of us are dreamers, while others are realists or critics. This can prevent us from seeing an issue from other angles. Walt Disney’s method uses all three of these thinking styles to help view a situation from different perspectives and find the best way forward.
2240: Problem solving, decision-making, and planning suffer when we have too narrow a focus, yet it can be difficult to change how we naturally approach issues. Using Disney’s three styles together will improve your decision-making.
2241: • The Dreamer is focused on potential and possibilities.• The Realist focuses on practical aspects and implementation.• The Critic questions and challenges plans and assumptions and notices potential problems or flaws.
2242: Using the Disney method
2243: Select an issue you want to address but put it to one side while you get into the right frame of mind.
2244: Go to three different places to think about the issue from each perspective (you will associate each environment with that approach). These can be entirely different places or simply different parts of one room.
2245: For each way of thinking (starting with Dreamer, moving to Realist and then to Critic), first remember a time when you were either creative, realistic, or critical. This will help you access that style and apply it to the current situation.
2246: In each frame of mind, address the issue at hand solely from that perspective.
2247: • In the Dreamer space, let your ideas flow freely.• In the Realist space, think about how the ideas you have created can be implemented. How can they be achieved? What needs to happen?• In the Critic space, question and challenge your ideas and plan. Identify strengths and weaknesses; look for flaws; look for gaps or potential problems. Determine what needs to be done better.
2248: Once you have completed these stages, go back to the beginning and re-evaluate your original dream and plan through each thinking stage in turn. You can repeat this process until you feel the plan works well from each perspective.
2249: Types of questions to ask at each stage
2250: Dreamer• Why am I doing this?• Can it be done better?• What would I like to happen?• Wouldn’t it be great if…?• What reward or result would I like?
2251: Realist• How can I make that happen?• Who else do I need to make it work?• What needs to happen – and when?• What resources do I need?• How much will it cost?
2252: Critic• Does the idea really have potential?• Is the objective achievable?• Are there any barriers or resource issues?• Does the plan work? Consider issues such as timing, cost, or market potential.• How can the plan be improved – are there gaps or are some things unnecessary?

2254: THE MATE MODEL FOR STRATEGIC SELLING
2255: Achieving your sales objectives
2256: Segmenting and managing your contacts within a client organization in terms of their support for your sales objectives is a highly effective way of developing client relationships and selling.
2257: Four steps
2258: Step 1: Define your unique sales objective.Be clear about what you are selling and when, and the value it brings. What makes it an attractive proposition? What is its value for the organization or client? This sounds simple but it can be muddled or overlooked, with disastrous consequences.
2259: Step 2: Identify all the players using the MATE model.MATE highlights the need to focus on Money, Allies, Technical experts, and End users. Identify each contact (including those you don’t know), recording their job title and name.
2260: MoneyThey have the ultimate veto on sales.
2261: AlliesThey provide useful information, can guide you, and influence others to support your objective.
2262: Technical experts / assessorsThey filter out information, can be gatekeepers, and can influence “Money”.
2263: End usersThey use, manage, or work with your products.
2264: • Money. The budget holder has authority over the decision to spend. They focus on the bottom line and have the power of veto. They ask: “What impact will this have and what return will we get?”• Allies / Advocates. They provide valuable information, lead you to the right people, and may be influential inside or outside the organization.• Technical experts. Gatekeepers who evaluate technical aspects. They can say “no” based on specifications.• End users. They judge how the proposal affects their work. They ask: “Will it work for me or my department?”
2265: Step 3: Consider each individual’s level of support.Assess whether their support for your sales objective is high, medium, or low.
2266: Step 4: Consider each individual’s level of influence.Assess their influence within the organization — high, medium, or low.
2267: Check for warning signs
2268: • Have I at least one person for each area?• Am I free from concerns about their influence?• Have I made personal contact with them?• Do I know their response modes and what they are looking for?
2269: Identify your tactics to further the sale and eliminate warning signs. Be honest and prepared to challenge and develop your thinking. With the information gathered, contact key people, establish rapport, and understand their needs.

2403: Surviving in the Ai Era
2404: With the rapid adopting of Ai which will result mass replacement of jobs - Your future role is to do everything AI cannot: to provide context, judgment, ethics, inspiration, and connection.
2405: The skills to develop are broken down into two categories:
2406: The "AI-Immune" Core SkillsThe "AI-Augmentation" Technical Skills.The "AI-Immune" Core Human Skills
2407: These are the skills that are incredibly difficult, if not impossible, to automate. They are the bedrock of your value.
2408: 1. Strategic Thinking & Critical Reasoning:
2409: • What it is:Moving beyond "what" to do and "how" to do it, to "why" we are doing it. Seeing the bigger picture, connecting disparate dots, and making judgments in ambiguous situations where data is incomplete or conflicting.
2410: • How to develop it:• Always ask "Why?" and "So what?" in meetings. Challenge assumptions (respectfully).• Get involved in strategic projects, even tangentially. Volunteer to help with business cases.• Read widely about industry trends, economics, and geopolitics to understand the external forces shaping your company.
2411: 2. Ethical Judgment & Nuanced Decision-Making:
2412: • What it is:AI can be trained on data, but it lacks a moral compass. You will be the one to make the call on layoffs, resource allocation, ethical dilemmas, and decisions that involve fairness, bias, and long-term consequences.
2413: • How to develop it:• Study ethics, especially in the context of AI and business.• When faced with a decision, explicitly list the ethical trade-offs.• Seek out mentors known for their strong judgment and ask them how they approach tough calls.
2414: 3. Empathy & Emotional Intelligence (EQ):
2415: • What it is:The ability to understand, connect with, and influence the emotions of your team members. This is crucial for motivation, conflict resolution, and fostering psychological safety.
2416: • How to develop it:• Practice active listening. Focus entirely on the speaker without formulating your response.• Learn to read non-verbal cues.• Regularly ask for feedback on your own management style and its impact on others.
2417: 4. Inspirational Leadership & Vision Casting:
2418: • What it is:AI can set a target; you can inspire people to want to hit it. You create a compelling vision, foster a positive culture, and motivate your team through purpose, not just process.
2419: • How to develop it:• Work on your storytelling skills. Learn to frame goals as a shared mission.• Public speaking and communication courses are invaluable.• Be transparent about challenges and celebrate wins genuinely.
2420: 5. Creativity & Complex Problem-Solving:
2421: • What it is:Not just coming up with ideas, but synthesizing novel solutions to messy, multi-faceted problems that have no precedent or clear answer.
2422: • How to develop it:• Practice brainstorming techniques and run workshops with your team.• Tackle problems from other departments to stretch your thinking.• Constantly ask, "What if we tried the opposite?" or "How would Company X in a different industry solve this?"
2423: The "AI-Augmentation" Skills to Pick Up a New Area
2424: These skills allow you to leverage AI, making you a more effective and valuable manager. This is your bridge to picking up new, high-value areas.
2425: 1. AI Literacy (The New Managerial Currency):
2426: • What it is:You don't need to be a data scientist, but you must understand what AI can and cannot do. Know the basics of machine learning, natural language processing, and generative AI. Understand concepts like "prompt engineering" to get the best out of tools like ChatGPT or Claude.
2427: • How to develop it:• Take online courses on AI for business (Coursera, edX, LinkedIn Learning have many).• Use AI tools daily for your own work (drafting emails, summarizing reports, brainstorming) to build an intuitive understanding.• Follow AI thought leaders and read reputable tech news sources.
2428: 2. Data Fluency, Not Just Data Literacy:
2429: • What it is:Beyond reading a dashboard, it's about knowing what questions to ask of the data, interpreting the results critically (is this correlation or causation?), and using data to tell a compelling story to drive action.
2430: • How to develop it:• Take a basic statistics course.• Always question the source and methodology behind the data you're given.• Practice creating presentations that use data to support a narrative.
2431: 3. Change Management & Ambiguity Navigation:
2432: • What it is:The business landscape will be in constant flux due to AI. Your role will be to guide your team through that change, managing fear, reskilling people, and maintaining morale during uncertainty.
2433: • How to develop it:• Volunteer to lead a small-scale change initiative.• Study change management models (e.g., ADKAR, Kotter's 8-Step).• Become comfortable making decisions with 70% of the information instead of 100%.
2434: How to Synthesize This to Pick Up a New Area
2435: Let's say you're a marketing manager and you want to move into Product Management, a field that heavily leverages AI.
2436: • Lead with Human Skills:Frame your value around your core strengths. "I excel at understanding customer pain points (Empathy), translating them into a compelling product vision (Strategic Thinking), and motivating a cross-functional team to build it (Leadership)."
2437: • Augment with AI Skills:Simultaneously, you learn the new domain. You use AI to analyze user feedback at scale, you study how top product managers use AI for roadmapping and A/B testing analysis (AI Literacy), and you learn the key metrics (DAU, MAU, Churn) that define the field (Data Fluency).
2438: • Become the Bridge:You position yourself as the person who can bridge the technical AI capabilities with the human needs of the customer and the business goals. This is an incredibly powerful and sought-after position.

What drives the economic engine? (Creating value that AI cannot.)d. The intersection of these three is where your unshakable career will be.e. Conclusion: Your job won't be "taken" by AI if you evolve your role. Become the manager who uses AI as the most powerful tool in your toolbox to amplify your team's uniquely human potential. Focus on being the strategist, the coach, the ethicist, and the innovator. The managers who thrive will be those who lead with AI, not against it.

3342: THE GROW MODEL FOR COACHING
3343: The single most important technique for executive coaching
3344: The GROW model, developed by Sir John Whitmore, provides a frame-work for coaching. GROW has four stages: Goals, Reality, Options and Way forward. Responsibility for setting goals rests with the coachee. The coach works in a non-directive way, supporting and challenging.
3345: GOALS
3346: This focuses on the coachee’s aims and priorities. It sets the agenda for the coaching conversation. The coach should be flexible and prepared to explore, question and challenge. This is achieved with questioning and empathy. The outcome is a clear set of goals for the session and the overall coaching relationship.
3347: Questions include:
3348: • What is your goal?• What are your priorities?• What are you trying to achieve?• How will you know when you have achieved it?• Is the goal specific and measurable?• How will you know when it has been achieved?• What will success look like?
3349: REALITY
3350: Explore the learner’s current position: the reality of their circumstances and their concerns relating to their goals. The coach needs to help the coachee analyze and understand the significant issues relating to their goal through intelligent questioning. The coach can also provide information and summarize the situation to clarify the reality.
3351: Questions include:
3352: • Can you control the result? What don’t you have control over?• What are the milestones or key points to achieving goals?• Who is involved and what effect could they have?• What have you done so far and what are the results?• What are the major issues you are encountering?
3353: OPTIONS
3354: The coach helps the coachee to generate options, strategies and action plans for achieving goals. This can uncover new aspects of the individual’s current position with the result that discussion reverts back to the coachee’s reality. This is fine if it is productive or enlightening – the aim is to help the individual, not rigidly follow a process.
3355: Questions include:
3356: • What options do you have? Which do you favour and why?• If you had unlimited resources, what options would you have?• Could you link your goal to another organizational issue?• What would be the perfect solution?
3357: WAY FORWARD
3358: Do not rush the final stage. The aim is to agree what needs to be done. It can help for the coachee to develop a practical plan to implement their option. The coach should be a sounding board, highlighting strengths and weaknesses, testing the approach and offering additional perspectives.
3359: Questions include
3360: • What are you going to do – and when? Who needs to know? What support and resources do you need?• How will you overcome obstacles and ensure success?
3361: Finally, the most effective plans incorporate a review and feedback process to check progress and provide motivation.

3363: DEVELOPING INFLUENCE AND ASSERTIVE LEADERSHIP
3364: Providing support and challenge while strengthening results and relationships.
3365: Whether you are giving feedback or selling a product or an idea, influencing requires an understanding of how your behaviour affects others.
3366: Overview
3367: All individuals have their own personality – the result both of nature and nurture – and this remains largely unchanging. However, behaviour is different: it is flexible and capable of being developed and enhanced. It’s useful to consider behaviour (yours and others) in terms of warmth or coldness, dominance or submissiveness.
3368: • Warm means being supportive, open, positive, empathetic, constructive and engaging – not simply ‘friendly’.• Cold means being suspicious, detached, not focused on people or relationships.• Dominant means being challenging, in control, confident, strong, authoritative and direct.• Submissive means subduing your own thoughts or actions for something or someone else.

3380: Avoiding: cold and submissive behaviour
3381: When dealing with avoiding behaviour, the first priority is to get people engaged. Useful techniques include displaying lower dominance and higher warmth, using open questions aimed at making them feel secure and softening body language and intonation while continuing to smile.

3388: WEEK 3
3389: VISIONING
3390: Creating your future
3391: By imagining the future you want and then translating those ideas into practical and actionable plans, you will make it happen.
3392: Orienting thinking towards the future is particularly important for middle and senior managers and leaders because it provides focus, determines the company’s culture, builds resilience and adaptability and engages employees.
3393: The need
3394: A powerful vision motivates and guides everyone at all levels in a company. People manage what is in front of them, as daily and short-term tasks understandably dominate our routine and thinking. This certainly keeps things running smoothly in the stable present but is ill suited to coping with change or taking advantage of (or creating) opportunities. Visioning liberates us from simply managing the present, achieving more of the same or being unprepared for new developments, and thus enables us to build a more successful future.
3395: The process
3396: Visioning involves assessing and challenging current thinking and methods, developing new ideas and deciding on the future you would like. It is also necessary to look outside your company – noticing and understanding trends, identifying threats and opportunities.
3397: It can be helpful to involve others in a visioning exercise by asking their views on various issues. These questions will prompt thinking and encourage each person to consider and challenge the company’s aims and activities and to suggest new options (giving reasons for their choices).
3398: Using these answers, you identify the most common issues and ideas, reduce these options to the ones that are most significant and then draft a provisional vision statement – this can be done by a smaller group of people, with the final vision being reviewed and approved by everyone involved. As well as generating ideas and opening up discussions, a major advantage of involving others in the visioning process is that you will gain their commitment to the final vision.
3399: Once you have developed your vision, determine how it can be achieved:
3400: • Deal with any barriers that may stand in the way and consider how future events may affect it.• Develop a practical plan and communicate the vision and plan to every-one – show people why it is important, what it will achieve and how it will work and gain their commitment.• To bring others with you, your vision needs to be clear, convincing, credible, easy to grasp, actionable, inspiring and focused – but not overly prescriptive, to provide flexibility and adaptability.
3401: What’s next?
3402: A vision is for nothing if it is not acted upon. You should ensure that all strategy and decisions are guided by the vision and that everyone remains committed to the vision. A vision also needs to be reviewed and adapted to changing circumstances to ensure that it remains relevant and useful.

3404: THE CHANGE CURVE
3405: Understanding how people respond to change
3406: The human reaction to change is now well understood. The change process is commonly understood by reference to the research on people’s reaction to bereavement. Elisabeth Kubler-Ross has been a great contributor to our understanding of the experience of loss and bereavement, as well as how we react to changes more generally. The stages of loss that people typically go through are now commonly known as the Change Curve.
3407: Overview
3408: Organizations often refer to the Change Curve in the context of job loss and redundancy. Dr. Kubler-Ross undertook her research on dying by interviewing terminally ill patients. Although this is one of the most extreme and disturbing changes that anyone can face, the reactions to it are the same as for many different types of change. There are several key stages that people go through, as shown in the graph below:
3409: Shock
3410: Denial
3411: Frustration and anger
3412: Depression
3413: Experiment and decision
3414: Acceptance and integration
3415: 1. Shock.The first reaction can often be shock – and all the emotion that results from this.
3416: 2. Denial.This is a typical reaction and it is important and necessary. It helps cushion the impact of the inevitability of change.
3417: 3. Frustration and anger.The person resents the change that they must face while others are less affected.
3418: 4. Depression.First, the person feels deep disappointment, perhaps a sense of personal failing, things not done, wrongs committed. Around this time they may also engage in bargaining: beginning to accept the change but striking bargains -for more time, for example,
3419: 5. Experiment and decision.Initial engagement with the new situation and learning how to work in the new situation, as well as making choices and decisions, and regaining control.
3420: 6. Acceptance and integration.Dr. Kubler-Ross describes this stage as neither happy nor unhappy. While it is devoid of feelings, it is not resignation – it is really a victory.
3421: People who are made redundant can go through a similar process. Just as with other types of change, people often go through a first stage before denial – that of shock or disbelief. We have witnessed people in shock following news of their redundancy. It can take a long time for people to reach the acceptance stage and often people oscillate between the different stages.

3429: WEEK 6
3430: EMPLOYEE ENGAGEMENT AND THE THREE-FACTOR THEORY
3431: The three things that matter most to people at work
3432: The factors that influence employee engagement combine in different ways and at different times for each person. Obviously, pay and leadership are important – with a direct relationship between pay and effort and the quality of leadership being critical to employee engagement. In addition, people like to do work that has meaning and purpose.
3433: Following international research, Sirota Consulting developed the Three-Factor Theory, addressing employee engagement by addressing three basic needs: equity, achievement and camaraderie.
3434: Leaders need to engage, inspire and energize their people. Gaining commitment and getting people to acquire new skills and achieve their full potential leads to ongoing improvements in performance, benefiting all concerned – individuals, teams and companies. The Three Factor Theory establishes a self-sustaining cycle of effective employee engagement by ensuring that practices and policies focus on equity, achievement and camaraderie.
3435: Equity
3436: People need to feel they are being treated fairly – especially in relation to others both inside and outside the company. This includes:
3437: • physical aspects – for example, working in a safe environment and being physically able to do a job• economic factors – people need to feel that their pay, benefits and job security are fair• psychological issues – including being treated with respect and consideration.
3438: Achievement
3439: People work better and achieve more if they believe in what they are doing and have confidence in the direction they are going. In short, they work best when they feel they are achieving something. Six issues influence this:
3440: Having challenging work and being able to use their skills
3441: Having the opportunity to develop their capabilities and to take risks
3442: Having the resources, authority, information and support to work effectively
3443: Knowing that the work is important and has value and purpose
3444: Receiving recognition – both financial and non-financial
3445: Having pride in the company’s aims, ethics, products and brand values.
3446: Camaraderie
3447: It is important for individuals to have good relations with co-workers. This requires congenial, co-operative, interesting and supportive relationships at all levels, with the most immediate ones being the most significant. This involves relationships:
3448: • with co-workers• within the business unit• across on-site departments• across the whole company.
3449: WEEK 7
3450: THE NINE PRINCIPLES OF MOTIVATION
3451: Creating the right environment
3452: So much in business depends on motivating others. There is only so much any one person can do, so getting the most out of others is crucial to success. This all begins with winning trust – everything else follows.
3453: Motivating others is an essential part of leadership. Your ability to motivate others relies on what they think of you and how they think you view them. This requires planning and vigilance and knowing that different people are motivated by different things. To motivate effectively, you need to know what motivates each person, the pressures they face, what influences their decisions and thinking, and how you can make a difference. These nine principles of motivation will help you to help your colleagues.
3454: 1. Be motivated yourself
3455: Self-motivation rallies others. People will ‘step up to the plate’ if you do so yourself. Knowing what motivates you will help you to motivate others.
3456: 2. Recruit people who are highly motivated and assign them to the right position
3457: Match people’s motivation to their job. Some are motivated by making sales while others are motivated by following processes, building teams or pursuing new ideas.
3458: 3. Treat people as individuals
3459: We all have different values and personalities. What works for one may not motivate another. So, tap into what motivates each individual to improve performance.
3460: 4. Set challenging but realistic targets
3461: Nothing is more demotivating than unachievable targets. Nothing is more motivating than achievable, we-can-beat-the-competition targets – they tap into our competitiveness and desire to produce something to be proud of.
3462: 5. Focus on progress – it motivates
3463: Everyone responds to a pat on the back – they’ve earned it and deserve it, so make it happen. The result: an upward spiral of people wanting to achieve more.
3464: 6. Develop an environment that motivates people
3465: Eliminate or minimize anything that blocks motivation – from bureaucracy and unnecessary procedures to lack of resources. Provide training and coaching to develop skills and to make people feel valued.
3466: 7. Ensure that people receive fair rewards
3467: Promotion, pay rises, sales commission, profit share, work benefits, additional responsibilities: these motivate people. They give people a reason to stay and to help your company excel.
3468: 8. Recognize people’s work
3469: We all want our efforts to be acknowledged. Recognition is needed to maintain commitment.
3470: 9. Be honest about your intent
3471: Honesty lies at the heart of motivation. Be clear about what your intentions are. People will be motivated only by those they can trust.

3474: Adapting your approach
3475: Situational leadership improves your ability to lead others and to respond effectively to situations.
3476: Different leadership styles
3477: By adjusting your style to match each challenge, you are more likely to achieve your desired outcome. To decide which approach is best, you need to consider the issues, what needs to happen and the people involved. To develop your situational leadership, you must be self-aware and understand your own style and how it impacts others.
3478: The model of situational leadership developed by Ken Blanchard and Spencer Johnson identifies and details the different leadership styles.

3484: Coaching ……….engaging
3485: Focuses on directing and supporting – using teaching and guiding skills

3503: THE JOHN WHITMORE MODEL
3504: Are you setting the right goals in the right way?
3505: Sir John Whitmore gave us the GROW model for coaching and he also highlighted a model for goal-setting that is SMART, PURE and CLEAR, ensuring that you and your colleagues have goals that are appropriate, achievable and successful.
3506: Goal-setting is vital whenever you need to focus someone (including yourself) on a specific objective or series of objectives – for example, at an annual appraisal, when someone starts a new role, or simply at the start of a new project.
3507: When developing people, it is important to provide a focus for action and to ensure a sense of purpose. This is the value of the John Whitmore model: it provides a checklist for goal-setting. So, when you are goal-setting, keep it simple and check that each goal meets the 14 criteria in Whitmore’s model.
3509: When goal-setting, distinguish between end goals and performance goals:
3510: • End goals are the ultimate objective. They could typically be to gain promotion or additional responsibility or to complete a major project (e.g. I need to achieve sales of £300,000 this year’).• Performance goals establish the level of performance that will help an individual to achieve their end goal. Performance goals include such elements as quality standards, time management and production targets (e.g. ‘I need to develop my negotiating skills’).
3511: Think about a current goal you have or one you want to address in the future. Answer the following questions to assess the robustness of your how approach to goal setting, monitoring and achievement. Also comment on how you could improve your approach.
3512: • What is your goal?• Is it specific? What, exactly, will success look like? Is it an end goal or a performance goal?• Is it measurable? How will progress be measured and monitored?• Is it attainable? Do you have the skills and resources needed?• How will you succeed and what will you do? What could go wrong? What are the risks?• Is it realistic? How does it relate to other people and activities? Are these links understood and could this goal benefit from other activities or expertise elsewhere in the organization?• What is the timescale? Are there milestones or dependencies in the plan?• Is the goal stated as positively as possible, in a way that will engage and encourage people?• Is it understood – is there a clear vision and view of what success will look like?• Is it relevant – how well does it relate to other issues and priorities?• Is it ethical?• Will it provide the right level of challenge?• Is it legal and are there legal (or regulatory) issues to consider?• Is it environmentally sound?• Is everyone agreed or is more agreement needed?• Has the goal been recorded and is it being monitored, with progress assessed and lessons learned?
3513: WEEK 10
3514: ACTION-CENTRED LEADERSHIP
3515: Managing the task, team and individual
3516: John Adair’s Action-Centred Leadership model views the role of leaders as integrating three areas: ensuring that the task, the team and the individual are working effectively and that their needs are met. Success relies on ensuring that all three responsibilities are mutually reinforcing.
3517: Overview
3518: As a leader, people look to you to set the direction, to support them, to help them achieve their goals, to ensure that team members work well together .and to make sure that the structures and procedures are in place (and working effectively). It is not enough to have a great idea; you are responsible for making it happen. In short, leadership is a total activity. If individuals aren’t motivated, teams will not function well; if teams don’t work well, tasks will fail and individual satisfaction falls, and so on. Whether you are leading one team, a business unit or an entire company, you need to provide for:
3519: • the needs of the task – provide the appropriate systems, procedures and structures• the needs of the team – promote team cohesiveness so that team members work well together• the needs of the individual – engage each person (by considering pay, their sense of purpose, their need to have achievements and contributions recognized, and their need for status and to be part of something that matters).
3520: A functional approach to leadership
3521: To provide for the needs of the task, team and individuals, John Adair out-lines eight leadership functions:
3522: Define the task. Everyone needs to understand what is expected, so be clear about the task at hand – make it SMART (Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Realistic and Time-constrained).
3523: Plan. Identify options, look for alternatives, make contingency plans and test your ideas. Working with others in a positive, open-minded, constructive and creative way will help you to develop the best plan.
3524: Brief others. To create the right conditions and bring people with you, you have to keep people informed. Both teams and individuals will work well only if they have access to information and your thinking – without open communication, confusion or even distrust can seriously hamper business strategy.
3525: Control effectively. You need self-control and you need to positively control others. Put the right procedures and monitoring in place, delegate tasks and trust others to both take responsibility and deliver results.
3526: Evaluate. Assess likely consequences, measure and judge the performance of both teams and individuals and provide necessary feedback and training.
3527: Motivate. Motivate yourself – if you are not motivated, it will be difficult to motivate others. Recruit people who are highly motivated. Set realistic and achievable targets – people respond to doable goal Focus on progress, reward success and recognize achievements.
3528: Be organized. Be organized yourself and ensure that teams and individuals have the necessary skills, procedures, structures and resources in place for them to do their jobs efficiently.
3529: Set the right example. The example you set to others influences their behaviour, motivation and willingness to follow you.
3530: WEEK 11
3531: THE SIX STEPS OF DELEGATION
3532: Developmental, productive – the cornerstone of leadership
3533: Without delegation, leaders cannot lead and managers cannot manage. Delegation develops skills, challenges and retains great people, and in-creases productivity. Yet many people have difficulty delegating. These six steps will help you to delegate effectively.
3534: Delegation requires empowerment and trust. You need to empower people give them the skills and confidence to act and take risks. You need to trust them and accept that mistakes will happen – mistakes that can be rectified and learned from and that are more than made up for by the progress that is achieved. Delegation is essential precisely because it goes directly to the bottom line – it has a huge impact on productivity, innovation and employee engagement and retention.
3535: Delegation can be learned but, to be successful, it rests entirely on having the right mindset. It is about bringing people with you. While experience helps, what is more important is attitude, good communication skills and confidence in yourself. These six stages provide a framework to help you delegate successfully:
3536: 1. Prepare to delegate
3537: Know what you want to achieve. Be clear about goals and priorities and decide how these can be achieved. Plan what needs to happen, and when, and bring people along with you. Winning hearts and minds and making sure people know the reasons for your plan and what is expected of them are essential.
3538: 2. Match the person to the task
3539: Know your people. Understand what they can do, their potential, what would challenge and stimulate them. It also helps to understand their future career plans. Make the most of each person’s abilities. Look for potential and take risks. With encouragement, training and trust, you will get more from each person.
3540: 3. Discuss and agree objectives
3541: Engage people with the task that needs to be completed. Everyone needs to understand your thinking, agree with the plan and be clear about what needs to be done and when. Consider constructive criticisms because it can improve your plan and gain the buy-in of others.
3542: 4. Put resources and power in place
3543: Provide the necessary resources and authority. In this way, your people can make decisions and act. Support your people whenever this is needed – they need to know you are behind them.
3544: 5. Monitor progress
3545: Ensure that people are accountable for delivering what is expected of them. Having overall goals and interim targets will help people to stay focused, to meet deadlines and to ensure that standards and results additional art met. The goal is to keep people motivated and on track and to provide support where needed.
3546: 6. Review progress
3547: Learn from experience and feedback. Compare and discuss results and objectives with those involved. Look at what worked well and what could have been done better. Use this to improve future plans.
3548: WEEK 12
3549: KOTTER’S EIGHT–STAGE PROCESS FOR LEADING CHANGE
3550: Achieving progress and getting the right things done in the best way possible
3551: The eight-stage process of creating major change was first outlined by John Kotter in his bestselling book Leading Change; it describes what the leader needs to do to ensure that beneficial change is achieved.
3552: 1. Establish a sense of urgency
3553: As a leader, you should initiate or take control of the process by emphasizing the need for change. The more urgent and pressing the need, the more likely people will be focused. Usually, the leader’s role is to stay positive and build on success. However, it can also help to emphasize failure – what might go wrong and how, when and what the consequences could be. You can also emphasize positive elements such as windows of opportunity that require swift and effective change.
3554: 2. Create the guiding coalition
3555: The guiding coalition needs to understand the purpose of the change process. Members should be united, coordinated and carry significant authority. The coalition needs to have the power to make things happen, to change systems and procedures, and to win people over.
3556: 3. Develop a vision and strategy
3557: The guiding coalition needs to create a simple, powerful vision that will direct and guide change and achieve goals. You need to develop a detailed strategy for achieving that vision. The strategy needs to be practical, work-able, understandable, simple and consistent.
3558: 4. Communicate the change vision
3559: Use every means possible to constantly communicate the new vision and strategies. This will build pressure, momentum and understanding, sustaining a sense of urgency. The guiding coalition should lead by example and act as role models for the behaviour expected of employees.
3560: 5. Empower broad-based action
3561: The leader and the guiding coalition cannot achieve change in isolation – it needs the commitment and effort of others. Provide a blame-free and supportive environment and empower your people by removing obstacles, changing systems or structures that undermine the vision and encouraging risk-taking and non-traditional ideas
3562: 6. Generate short-term wins
3563: These produce momentum and provide an opportunity to build on success. To do this, plan for visible improvements in performance – or ‘wins’, create those wins and recognize and reward people who make wins possible.
3564: 7. Consolidate gains and produce more change
3565: Once the excitement of the start-up phase has passed, the successes have been built and people know what is needed, people can tire and problems can arise. The key is to move steadily: maintain momentum without moving too fast. You need to continue by using increased credibility and understanding of what is still needed, hiring, promoting and developing people who can implement the changes and reinvigorating the process with new projects, themes and change agents.
3566: 8. Anchor new approaches in the organization’s culture
3567: A key danger in managing change is to finish too early. The best situation is often where change, development and continuous improvements become the norm. What matters is making changes that are firmly grounded in the organization. This requires you to explain the connections between the new behaviours or actions and success.
3568: WEEK 13
3569: SIX PRINCIPLES FOR GAINING COMMITMENT
3570: Achieving employee engagement during times of transition
3571: What is the goal of employee engagement? Quite simply: to maximize performance and profit. These will not happen if leaders don’t have their people’s commitment. Gone are the times when leaders simply informed others; nowadays a dialogue needs to take place. People need to feel valued and listened to, and leaders need to inspire, win hearts and minds, and harness talent and potential.
3572: Successful transitions depend on gaining commitment. Without it, companies underperform and strategy is harder to achieve. John Smythe developed six principles to engage employees – releasing creativity, raising productivity and promoting commitment and loyalty. They give people a compelling reason to work for you, to excel, and to implement plans successfully. By listening, engaging, empowering and encouraging people to share ideas, you will build confidence, loyalty and camaraderie.
3573: 1. Develop the right plan and make sure that everyone agrees
3574: Ensure that the senior team has explored all options and developed the best strategy. While teams often agree on a plan, some people may have held back ideas or not been on board. Making sure that everyone at the senior level is on board is critical.
3575: 2. Plan the transition process and prepare a timeline
3576: When planning the timeline for implementation, consider the timing of all demands that will be placed on people, including emotional and motivational aspects.
3577: 3. Decide who is to be involved – and how
3578: Make sure that everyone is clear about who is involved and how and why they are involved – or affected. When people know what their role is and understand your strategy, they are more engaged, adaptable and committed.
3579: 4. Set standards (including role modeling and measuring progress)

3674: THE 7S MODEL
3675: Assessing business performance
3676: The 7S model is a framework for assessing the performance of a company. It views all seven elements as equally important because they impact on each other – with failure in one area undermining the others. By appreciating how they are related, and assessing performance from this perspective, companies and teams can better align activities to achieve goals.
3677: Overview
3678: First developed in the 1970s by McKinsey and refined by Tom Peters, Robert Waterman and Richard Pascale, the 75 model works from the principle that success relies on simultaneously pursuing a combination of seven hard and soft aspects of running a business. Known for changing people’s thinking at the time, it still provides a useful framework for assessing and improving a company or how a team is working – identifying gaps and enabling adjustments to be made to ensure that all seven aspects are aligned, working together, and supporting and reinforcing one another. By knowing how things are interrelated, the framework raises awareness of the full impact of any changes.
3679: 1. StrategyThese are plans that determine, define and outline how to fulfill the company’s goals and purpose and to achieve competitive advantage.
3680: 2. StructureThis is how the company is organized and how each part relates to the others.
3681: 3. SystemsThis is about how both formal and informal business processes function.
3682: 4. Shared values (superordirlate goals)These are the company’s beliefs, values and guiding mission that draw people together and that directly influence their approach, thinking and actions.
3683: 5. SkillsThese are the capabilities of both the people and the organization.
3684: 6. StaffThis concerns the nature, type and general abilities of the people employed.
3685: 7. StyleThis is the organization’s culture and style of leadership that, along with having an internal impact, determine how people outside the organization view the company.
3686: The main point is that all seven elements are interrelated, with each affecting the others. In this, it can be viewed as an early proponent of holistic business. Significantly – and this is of particular relevance to leaders today – it reveals how underperformance can be attributed to neglect in any one of the seven aspects, regardless of strong focus and capabilities in one or more of the others. Richard Pascale subsequently argued that, while it is generally important to view all seven as equally significant to achieving success, having shared values (superordinate goals) is the element that binds all the others together.

3688: THE RULE OF 150
3689: A bold way to create the right working conditions.
3690: This rule is about limiting the number of people at any one location to 150.
3691: Overview
3692: The rule is based on the idea that 150 is the largest group size that people can deal with – beyond that number, it is increasingly difficult to form bonds with others. If groups are larger, hierarchies, regulations and formal measures are required. However, with fewer than 150, goals can be achieved informally and people work better and are happier, more motivated and more productive.
3693: Why it works
3694: Co-workers find socializing, teamworking, innovating, collaborating and sharing knowledge easier to achieve in groups of fewer than 150 people. By organizing operations into smaller groups, large companies can gain the benefit of smaller groups – being closer, driven, entrepreneurial, supportive and productive.
3695: The rule in practice
3696: Gore Associates, a high-tech firm, uses this rule. It has 15 plants all within 20 kilometres (12 miles) of one another, and each with fewer than 150 employees. It has resisted the option of merging its separate sites – despite potential cost savings – because the small size of each unit ensures that everyone knows everyone else and works well together.
3697: By organizing itself in this way, Gore, despite being a large company with thousands of employees, is still able to enjoy the entrepreneurial approach of a small start-up. Each unit enjoys the benefits of collective management, which are
3698: • improved communication• greater initiative• flexibility.
3699: It is notable that employee turnover is significantly less than the industry average and the company has enjoyed sustained profitability and growth for over 35 years.
3700: This does not mean that Gore has no control or input. It has put a strong managerial system in place to oversee each unit, to ensure that activities are coordinated and efficient. The company also encourages a sense of community and teamwork within these groups – after all, the rule only means that it is possible for workers to form positive bonds with each other, so efforts must still be made to ensure that this happens. In addition, Gore makes sure that it develops a sense of community across the company by encouraging people to communicate and collaborate with workers from other groups.WEEK 22

3714: UNDERSTANDING AND AVOIDING INERTIA
3715: When success traps us in the past
3716: It might seem counterintuitive to warn people about the dangers of success but that is exactly what Donald Sull did when he developed the concept of ‘active inertia’ – where people repeat the strategies and activities that have worked well in the past.
3717: A reliance on previous thinking and approaches – the formula of success – can cause a company to fail to respond properly to new developments. By applying past approaches to new conditions, the end result can be a downward spiral – leaving an organization vulnerable to more dynamic companies with approaches better suited to the new environment.
3718: How active inertia works
3719: A firm correctly discerns gradual shifts and developments in the external environment, but fails to respond effectively.
3720: Managers get trapped by success, often responding to the most disruptive changes by accelerating activities that succeeded in the past.
3721: The source of active inertia is a company’s success formula, the unique set of strategic frames, resources, processes, relationships and values that collectively influence managers’ actions.
3722: With time and repetition, people stop considering alternatives to their formula. The individual components of the success formula grow less flexible.
3723: How active inertia happens
3724: Active inertia occurs because people come to rely on a past formula of success, where accepted approaches become entrenched and people stop considering alternatives. Consequently, people continue to respond to external changes by pursuing fixes and activities that worked in the past. However, these responses are likely to be ineffectual because they are based on past success and not current and future needs.
3725: Why a past success formula does not guarantee a successful future
3726: Essentially, like it or not, our brains are lazy – subconsciously preferring the easy route to solving problems and then, equally subconsciously, superimposing a solid layer of reasons to justify our decisions. So it is hardly surprising that our brains fool us into being happy to rely on approaches that have proven successful in the past: it is easy and we have a ready-made wall of rock-solid excuses to hand.
3727: As individuals, our thinking, strategies, Methods, use of resources, relationships and values all become firmly entrenched. The consequence for companies is that this formula becomes so deeply embedded that they are left vulnerable when faced with changing conditions.
3728: It is understandable that past approaches should be so revered and relied upon – they are, after all, the reason for the company’s current success. However, we should keep in mind that this formula is exactly that: suited to the current, stable situation – not the future. Companies can suddenly find themselves commercially stranded.
3729: The bottom line is that, when faced with new developments, your approach needs to change accordingly – essentially, the survival of the fittest depends on adaptation.WEEK 24

3764: THE PARETO PRINCIPLE
3765: Finding the right locus and answer using the 80:20 rule
3766: Pareto analysis arose from Vilfredo Pareto’s observation that many activities break down into an 80:20 ratio, where 80 per cent of output is due to 20 per cent of the contributory factors. This observation is now used to focus business strategy, problem-solving and operations on the key inputs that are responsible for 80 per cent of the outcome.
3767: How it works
3768: The 80:20 ratio applies both to positive and negative situations, providing a useful means of dealing quickly with problems or opportunities. In other words, by identifying the small number of key factors that are contributing most to a situation, we can better focus efforts to achieve the desired result.
3769: Pareto analysis is only as good as the data that is used, so we need to ensure that all contributory factors are identified and that appropriate and revealing parameters and measures are established and interpreted correctly. Although not everything falls neatly into an 80:20 rule, Pareto analysis is still useful for identifying the main causal factors.
3770: This simple example shows how the process works
3771: Research and discuss the issue, identifying all contributory factors.
3772: Decide an appropriate time period and method of measurement.
3773: Measure how frequently each factor occurs (or another measure, such as cost).
3774: Rank the factors in descending order, with the largest one first.
3775: Calculate the frequency of each factor as a percentage of the total occurrences (or cost).
3776: Calculate the cumulative percentage (current percentage plus all previous percentages).
3777: Depict this information on a graph – with ‘frequency as a percentage of total’ as a bar chart and ‘cumulative percentage’ as a line, adding a third line showing the 80 per cent cut-off point.
3778: All factors that appear to the left of the intersection of the two lines are the ones contributing to 80 per cent of the result – these are the factors to focus on.

3780: BLUE OCEAN STRATEGYCreating unique market opportunities
3781: A Blue Ocean Strategy is one where the key to success Lies not in competing directly with rivals within a market, but in creating an entirely new market where there are currently no competitors and where the potential for high returns is vast.
3782: Developed by W. Chan Kim and Renee Mauborgne, Blue Ocean Strategy involves a change in strategic thinking towards a mindset that challenges existing market boundaries, rewrites the rules of competition, and creates a new, as yet uncontested, market space. The theory outlines two attitudes to competition: Red Oceans and Blue Oceans.
3783: The current marketplace for all products and services is made up of Red Oceans (bloody battlegrounds), where boundaries are clearly defined and companies operate within the boundaries of their accepted Red Ocean markets. Here, the entrenched battleground is one where companies compete to gain extra market share within the current market boundary.
3784: A very different attitude pervades the Blue Oceans. These are areas of deep, uncharted, almost limitless potential where the aim is not to compete on traditional grounds but to develop products and services that create entirely new markets. In essence, it is creating customers that do not yet exist.
3785: At its core, Blue Ocean Strategy believes that it is better to create tomorrow’s customers through developing a new market rather than scrabbling around trying to capture existing customers in the current marketplace. There may be many justifications for this approach but, quite simply, the reason seems straightforward: to create a monopoly situation and reap the high rewards before competitors enter the new market.
3786: Value creation
3787: Value is achieved by integrating the utility of the product with its cost and price. It is not a case of choosing between competing through managing costs or product differentiation: it is about pursuing both. It is this that creates the value that appeals across customer groups, drawing them into a new market. Think of this as maximizing the gap between the utility of the product and its price (facilitated by lower costs) – the larger this gap, the higher the value and the more it attracts customers.
3788: Blue Ocean Strategy relies on four main principles:
3789: Challenging existing market boundaries.Reconstruct the marketplace, identifying and creating new markets and customers. The Blue Ocean is a vast place where demand is unrealized – it doesn’t yet exist. The aim is to bring this demand into existence.
3790: Keeping focused on the overall picture.Be clear about your goals: what matters and needs to be achieved.
3791: Minimizing risk.Assess current industry standards and decide what can be:a. eliminated – things that are not necessaryb. reduced – things that do not need to be done to a high standardc. raised – things that should be done betterd. created – things that have never been offered before.

3809: THE PRODUCT LIFE CYCLEManaging your product portfolio
3810: From development and launch, through its peak to eventual decline, a product’s life cycle determines the strategy needed to optimize its return at each stage and to develop further products to ensure ongoing profit-ability and competitiveness.
3811: Although not an exact science, the duration of each stage varies according to the product and the markets involved. Some life cycles are obviously shorter than others – such as technology products. With very short life cycles, it is essential to maximize returns as quickly as possible and to be continually developing the next products. A long-lasting branded product, despite undergoing many life cycles, enjoys continuity from its brand name. Companies, however, still have to manage the life cycles of such branded products – planning the next improvement and managing the replacement of the current version.
3812: There are five stages in the product life cycle:
3813: Development – this includes entirely new products and changes or improvements to existing products
3814: Introduction – at this stage, costs can be high relative to revenue
3815: Growth – revenue rises and offsets costs
3816: Maturity – growth slows and competition rises
3817: Decline – sales decline due to increased competition or changing customer preferences
3818: The following describes tactics appropriate to each stage:
3819: DevelopmentDevelopment can be very costly, with unexpected delays, so cash-flow issues are paramount. Researching what customers are looking for and testing prototypes with potential customers will help you develop the right products with fewer glitches – as well as promoting a ready-made pool of customers. Importantly, product development is an ongoing process, ensuring that new products or improvements to existing products are ready to replace current products.
3820: IntroductionGetting the launch right is essential. Raising product awareness quickly requires promotional and advertising investment – depending on the nature of the product, targeting early adopters can be useful at this stage. An aggressive pricing strategy can achieve fast market penetration – although this will depend on the brand’s attributes. You could also consider minimizing distribution costs by limiting the availability of the product.
3821: GrowthIn the face of more competition, but still with considerable potential revenue and falling unit costs, strategy needs to focus on outcompeting rivals, delivering extra value to customers and increasing market share. Further promotional offers, marketing and advertising campaigns, attractive prices and promoting the product’s brand will strengthen your position.
3822: MaturityGiven the influx of competitors, a company is faced with several strategic options to strengthen its market share, including: product differentiation, entering new markets, attracting rivals’ customers, a price war, and reducing costs to maintain competitive pricing and profitability. It is important at this stage to monitor the financial situation and the viability of the different options.
3823: DeclineWith falling sales and reduced margins, any plans and further investment should be considered carefully. Reducing the available options for the product and reducing the number of markets the product is offered in will re-duce costs. Catering to your core customers to cement their loyalty can also boost profits at this stage. Other tactics to extend the life of a product include product extensions and entering previously untapped markets.

3825: SYSTEMS THINKINGBuilding better companies
3826: A company is a collection of systems, and systems within systems. These all need to operate individually and collectively, to drive the business forward. A company’s systems need to work with strategy, and they need to be open, adaptive and understood.
3827: Traditional approaches to strategy have emphasized the mechanics of how things work. This can result in too much complexity and ‘over-engineering’, with processes and systems being overly focused on the present, unable to adapt and failing to win people over. The fundamental flaw is setting a predetermined solution at the start of any redesign, which then influences subsequent thinking, narrowing views and ambitions, and misses better options. Often, the result of re-engineering is an expensive disappointment.
3828: In The Fifth Discipline, Peter Senge revolutionized business re-engineering by arguing that solutions should be considered only after fully understanding the relationships within and between systems (including the behaviours involved) and examining all related problems and issues. Essentially: go back to basics, look deeper and search further, before you start thinking about solutions. Such open systems thinking builds teams, promotes creativity and develops new approaches. It works with the company’s long-term strategy, enabling adaptability and continual improvement. It is not the easiest approach: it is time-consuming and mentally demanding and generates an overwhelming number of questions. It works best when the right culture and mindset exist.
3829: There are seven steps to successful systems thinking:
3830: Explore the situationGather the information you need without making judgments or looking for causes and effects. At this stage, do two things:• Cast your net wide, collating as much information as possible.• Be objective and detached (see things as they are, without an agenda).
3831: Describe the systemTo understand what you are dealing with, list and describe the things that have happened – including the culture, people and atmosphere. Identify, date and examine trends and patterns. Position each factor on a diagram to show the relationships that exist between them. This highlights how aspects work together and reveals negative and positive feedback loops to enable you to analyze the systems in more depth later.
3832: Build modelsMathematical and IT tools are useful but they will take you only so far because systems need to be considered as they really function if they are to be understood and improved.
3833: Compare your model to what is actually happeningCheck your model against reality to see whether it fits and whether you have understood it correctly or have missed something.
3834: Identify potential improvementsOnce you have confirmed that your model is an accurate representation of what is happening, explore ways in which the system can be improved.
3835: Implement your improvementsMonitor changes and identify any further improvements that could be made. It is essential to win people over – successful change depends on people’s willingness to work positively with the new systems.
3836: Repeat the processSystems thinking is a continuous activity; companies need to adapt to change and to take advantage of new opportunities.

3838: MARKET BARRIERSProtecting your profits
3839: Market exit and entry barriers have both positive and negative effects on profit, depending on your company’s position and on the impact the barriers have on your competitors. A key aspect of awareness of market barriers is that they increase our focus on external issues. In short, it forces us to look up and see the business horizon in much greater detail.
3840: Overview
3841: The word ‘barrier’ is slightly misleading. While barriers will certainly make you do your sums, consider the ramifications and prepare contingency plans, they also deter your competitors. And that is the point: use barriers to your advantage. Your strategy must include careful calculations about the costs involved and you must balance these against the revenue and market dominance potential, but it should also look for how to exploit barriers to your advantage.
3842: The matrix below summarizes the impact of barriers to entry and exit on profitability.
3843: Low entry barriers – Returns: stable | Profit: lowLow entry barriers – Returns: at risk | Profit: low
3844: High entry barriers – Returns: stable | Profit: highHigh entry barriers – Returns: at risk | Profit: high
3845: Low exit barriers | High exit barriers
3846: Entry barriers
3847: There are many barriers to entry, including:• the high cost of capital• other companies owning patents and proprietary technology• high research and development costs of developing necessary products• expensive technology• existing companies enjoying economies of scale that you can’t afford to match• a restricted number of government licences• the expense of (or lack of access to) effective distribution channels• Your product not being different enough from market leaders.
3848: Exit barriers
3849: There are many exit barriers, including:• high fixed costs• few buyers for your expensive, specialized equipment• contractual salary, redundancy and pension commitments• legal regulations• outstanding contractual obligations• being tied to other companies• risk to brand image.
3850: Not only do you need to understand all the costs, legalities and brand issues, you need to understand how barriers work: how they affect you and, importantly, how they will affect your current and potential competitors. Do this and you will determine the business strategy that is right for your company.
3851: For example, the ideal scenario for an established company is to have high entry barriers and low exit barriers. The reasons are self-evident: high entry barriers deter others from entering the market you are already operating in; low exit barriers will not cause you a problem should you decide to change course.
3852: A much less favourable scenario is having low entry barriers but high exit barriers. Obviously, with low entry barriers, competitors can flood into the market. Unfortunately, the high exit barriers will make it difficult and ex-pensive to leave the market, restricting your strategic options in the future.WEEK 32

3866: PORTER’S GENERIC COMPETITIVE STRATEGIESChoosing the road ahead
3867: Porter’s Generic Competitive Strategies describe how a company develops competitive advantage across its chosen market. There are three generic strategies: cost leadership, differentiation and focus.
3868: Overview
3869: A company chooses to pursue one of two types of competitive advantage: either with lower costs than its competitors, or by differentiating itself along dimensions valued by customers so it can command a higher price. A company also chooses one of two types of scope: either focus (offering its products to selected segments of the market) or industry-wide, offering its product across many market segments. The generic strategy reflects choices made about both the type of competitive advantage and the scope. The concept was first described by Michael Porter in 1980.
3870: Cost leadership
3871: The strategic aim is to offer competitive prices by reducing costs and to also use lower costs to raise profit margins, fund discount campaigns, or launch an aggressive price war to gain market share and eliminate the competition. Reducing costs can also open up new markets that were less able to sustain higher prices. Another advantage of lowering costs is providing flexibility should suppliers raise prices unexpectedly and suddenly, without you also having to raise prices.
3872: The risks, however, are that other companies can copy your methods, eroding any advantage you have, and the lack of investment in research and development will leave your products looking dated and inefficient compared to those of competitors with better equipment and methods.
3873: Differentiation
3874: Developing distinctive products for different segments separates you from the competition. It creates product desirability, strengthens your brand, promotes customer loyalty, provides competitive advantage, enables higher prices and delivers higher returns. Your products can be differentiated from those of your competitors but you can also differentiate your own products from one another to target different customer groups and markets.
3875: The risks are higher costs and waste and the potential for more complex operations.
3876: Focus
3877: While focus incorporates aspects of cost leadership and differentiation, it is concerned with targeting products and services at one market segment, gaining increased share in that segment. The risk is that this will produce a narrow view that is overly focused on the short term, on too few factors, and on a less lucrative or unstable market and thus fails to see potential elsewhere.

3925: RESOURCES AND THE CRITICAL PATHThe drivers of business performance
3926: ‘Resources’ is an overused term in business but any factor providing value or benefit, from whatever origin, is a resource that can be used to benefit the business. Increasing and strengthening resources over time can be seen as the critical path to business success.
3927: Managing resources
3928: Assessing which resources are important involves taking a view across the whole of the business and identifying those factors, direct or indirect, tangible or intangible, that can be expanded and used for competitive advantage. Understanding which resources are most important and how they should be managed requires a clear understanding of the nature of each resource, in terms of the following:
3929: • The interaction between resources. Resources can combine in a cycle to accelerate their growth. For example, rising sales volumes may lead to more cash and more internal capacity, both of which can be used to generate increasing sales, perhaps by entering new markets, in a self-sustaining cycle. Similarly, product quality (an intangible resource) may lead to increased sales, and this in turn can generate sufficient cash to continue improving product quality (and continue increasing sales). In the same way that resources can interact to reinforce one another, they can also interact by limiting one another.
3930: • The fragility of the resource. Cash, quality, customers, staff, reputation and most other resources can all disappear with remarkable speed and ease. It is, therefore, important to control the main factors likely to damage or undermine resources. For example: cash needs to be monitored and controlled; quality can be eroded by suppliers; service can be undermined by the attitudes of personnel; and brand reputation may be damaged by the actions of distributors.
3931: • The quality of resources. It is worth considering how the quality of re-sources can be developed. For example, a customer base is a valuable re-source, but its quality might be improved by increasing customer loyalty to your brand – for instance, by using customer loyalty schemes.
3932: How resources affect performance
3933: Resources have a special characteristic: they fill and drain over time. Since a firm’s performance at any time directly reflects the resources available, it is essential that we understand how those resources develop over time and how we can control that process. To build strong business performance, we need to know:
3934: • how many resources are available• how fast these numbers are changing• how strongly these factors are being influenced by things under our control and by other forces• how resources interrelate with one another.
3935: In a system where resources are integrated and working together, what matters is not the uniqueness of individual resources but how they combine and work together to deliver value for customers. To manage resources and ensure that they drive performance in the desired direction, start by understanding how resources work together.

3937: DEVELOPING INTANGIBLE RESOURCESIntangibles: what they are, why they matter, and what they can do for you
3938: Soft ‘intangible’ factors can play a crucial role in developing a business’s competitive performance. For example, a charity with strong commitment from its donors will achieve its goals more easily, and a business with a culture that encourages coaching, risk-taking, new ideas and avoids blame is more likely to make improvements and achieve progress.
3939: Unfortunately, intangibles can be tough to manage. You may easily borrow cash, buy production capacity or hire staff, but it is slow and difficult to build staff morale, a strong reputation, support from a charity’s donors or to generate new ideas.
3940: Overview
3941: Resources can typically be classified into two of four categories: either direct or indirect and tangible or intangible.
3942: • Direct resources are those factors such as staff expertise, cash or intellectual property that can be developed and nurtured by the business. Customers are, perhaps, the biggest single direct resource. (Viewing customers as a resource focuses thinking on how to accumulate and retain them.)
3943: • Indirect resources are those factors that have a bearing on the quality, strength and value of resources. For example, effective training and development policies are an indirect resource, as they build the effectiveness of staff expertise.
3944: • Tangible resources are those that can be physically seen, such as cash, inventory, sales volumes and customers; typically, these have the highest profile within the organization, as they are the most apparent.
3945: • Intangible resources such as service quality, brand reputation or staff expertise are also vitally important to success.
3946: Of these, intangible resources can be the hardest to manage (and the easiest to ignore). Several techniques will help ensure that intangible resources are working well with the rest of the business:
3947: • Identify the most important intangibles. Since your performance relies on concrete resources, assess whether an intangible factor is likely to influence your ability to win or lose the resources. It is not advisable to waste time examining too many factors, as it is more likely that only one or two factors will have a significant impact.
3948: • Be clear which of these factors genuinely ‘accumulate’ through time and which are simply current features of the business. ‘Quality’ and ‘service’ reflect the balance between what has to be done and what is available to do it, in which case they do not accumulate. Reputation, motivation, commitment and relationships, on the other hand, are built up and drain away over time in response to events.
3949: • Assess intangibles carefully, identif9 the best measure and also the events causing each intangible to rise or fall. Look for ways to strengthen intangibles.
3950: • Build intangible measures into your performance tracking system. Reporting systems now commonly incorporate soft measures (as distinct from hard data, such as financial measures) from various parts of the organization, recognizing that soft measures such as engagement or reputation are crucial to a well-performing system.
3951: • If you don’t know, don’t ignore the issue. Soft factors are influencing your organization, continually and powerfully. Remember, if you choose to ignore them, you are not, in fact, really leaving them out. Instead, you are assuming that they are satisfactory and unchanging. This is unlikely to remain correct, so make your best estimate and start tracking and understanding them.

3986: DEEP DIVE PROTOTYPING
3987: Developing creative, practical solutions
3988: Developed and popularized by the consultancy firm IDEO, Deep Dive Prototyping is a focused, team-based approach to generating solutions to a particular problem or challenge. It is a useful way of stimulating creative thinking and to capture and fine-tune ideas.
3989: The process
3990: A deep dive combines brainstorming and prototyping (building and exploring a potential solution) to devise actions that will help move a business forward. There is no time limit, and the main stages are:
3991: • Build a team that has a mix of strengths and approaches.• Define the design challenge – to do this, understand your market, customers, technology and constraints and use this information to develop key themes.• Visit experts, and gather information on markets, customers – and ideas generally.• Share ideas.• Brainstorm and vote – this involves intensive brainstorming and discussion to imagine new concepts and ideas based around the main themes.• Develop a fast prototype.• Test and refine the prototype, streamlining ideas to improve the proto-type and to overcome obstacles – at this stage, evaluate and prioritize ideas and decide how they can be implemented.• Focus on the prototype and produce a final solution.• Give credit to those involved – this promotes motivation and encourages continued innovative thinking.WEEK 43
3992: DEVELOPING CREATIVE THINKINGMaking creativity the norm
3993: Edward de Bono sees creativity as a learnable skill, one that is best harnessed through formal techniques. He proposes that parallel thinking is a more useful and effective means of putting creative talent to work.
3994: Formal creativity works because it works with the way everyone’s brains work: both consciously and subconsciously, we automatically filter, categorize, process and organize information. Building on this, de Bono argues that parallel thinking is more effective for generating the results that make a difference to companies. (Parallel thinking is when each individual puts forward their own thoughts in parallel with those of others. In this way, each individual is able to complement, enrich and build on one another’s thinking, rather than competing or attacking the thoughts of others.)
3995: The reason why this is more important than ever is because what companies previously relied on for competitive advantage – competence, information and technology – are now easy-to-obtain commodities. These are all buyable commodities, enabling your competitors to rapidly erode any advantage you may have had. Today, what matters is creating value from these commodities.
3996: Understanding creativity
3997: Creativity solves problems, challenges existing methods, and provides a better and constantly improving way forward. Given the reward, companies need to know how best to harness creativity in a way that is useful. A major flaw in traditional brainstorming is that it assumes that, if you give people the freedom to express themselves, they will magically become creative. This is not the case. For organizations, useful creativity needs to be a formal activity that requires thinking that provokes and challenges a current situation and then searches for answers.
3998: Provoke, challenge and search for solutions
3999: Given the brain’s natural inclination to organize information and think laterally, we can tackle issues by simply taking a random starting point. Our brains will automatically process information, make connections and point us in new directions. Allowing such randomness in selecting a starting point is important. It suggests new possibilities and takes thinking along new paths. Significantly, it is likely that our brains have already processed information and are subconsciously suggesting such opening gambits because they could be highly relevant. This serves to break us out of the current doldrums and set us on a new course.
4000: Next, our new thinking needs to move forward: to challenge the information it is processing. Just because something has always been done a certain way does not mean it is carved in stone: methods can always be improved upon. Constantly questioning and challenging is a mindset that is a huge source of competitive advantage precisely because it is the way that companies create value from their resources. An important point to remember is that even when something seems to be working and is successful it doesn’t mean it is the best that it can be. Once thinking challenges the norm, we will automatically explore alternative and potentially better solutions.

4003: THE DISCOVERY CYCLE (ORCA)Evaluating innovations
4004: Discovery – making things known or visible – is a vital precursor for innovation. The Discovery Cycle is a way of choosing new ideas that are profitable and scalable.
4005: The Discovery Cycle has four stages, summarized in the acronym ORCA:
4006: Observation. Understand how the world is changing – for example, by looking for anomalies, paradoxes, peripheral developments and direct experience.
4007: Reflection. Techniques that work best at this stage include zooming in and out, using a muse, suspending judgement, slowing down, reflecting on what’s missing, restructuring data to simplify patterns, juxtaposing pieces of different information (bisociation) and taking time to rest.
4008: Conversation. People set the pace and scope for innovation, so the best techniques to use at this stage include contrasting views, setting the agenda, framing the issues and generating hypotheses.
4009: Analysis. The final stage of the Discovery Cycle involves gathering systematic evidence, classifying and categorizing data, naming, completing data analysis and hypothesizing.

4027: THE SIX THINKING HATSIf you want to get ahead, get a hat
4028: Created by Edward de Bono, the Six Thinking Hats technique explains the different styles of thinking we use while making decisions.
4029: Overview
4030: People usually have a preferred thinking style. While this style may be useful, it can limit thinking and cause us to miss better solutions. The Six Thinking Hats method allows us to deliberately switch thinking styles so that problems are viewed from multiple perspectives. This leads to better, more balanced decisions.
4031: Using only one approach, such as logical thinking, may ignore emotional reactions, risks, or creative possibilities. Applying multiple hats gives a fuller picture and improves both decision-making and implementation.
4032: The Six Thinking Hats
4033: White HatFocuses on facts, data, and information.Looks at what information is available and what is missing.Analyzes trends, evidence, and objective data.
4034: Red HatUses intuition, emotions, and gut feelings.Considers how people may emotionally react to a decision.Allows expression of feelings without justification.
4035: Black HatLooks at risks, problems, and weaknesses.Asks why an idea might fail.Helps identify dangers and prepare contingency plans.
4036: Yellow HatFocuses on optimism and benefits.Looks at value, advantages, and positive outcomes.Helps maintain motivation during challenges.
4037: Green HatEncourages creativity and new ideas.Explores alternatives and innovative solutions.Free-flowing thinking with minimal criticism.
4038: Blue HatFocuses on control, structure, and process.Commonly used by facilitators or leaders.Manages discussion flow and ensures productive thinking.

4061: DISNEY’S CREATIVITY STRATEGYWhen you need more than just the bare necessities
4062: We all have a preferred thinking style – some of us are dreamers, while others are realists or critics. This can prevent us seeing an issue from other angles. Walt Disney’s method uses all three of these thinking styles to help view a situation from different perspectives and find the best way forward.
4063: Problem solving, decision-making and planning suffer when we have too narrow a focus, yet it can be difficult to change how we naturally approach issues. Using Disney’s three styles together will improve your decision-making.
4064: • The Dreamer, who is a dreamer, is focused on potential and possibilities.• The Realist focuses on practical aspects and implementation.• The Critic questions and challenges plans and assumptions, and notices potential problems or flaws.
4065: Using the Disney method
4066: Select an issue you want to address but put it to one side while you get into the right frame of mind.
4067: Go to three different places to think about the issue from each perspective (you will associate each environment with that approach). These can be entirely different places or simply different parts of one room.
4068: For each way of thinking (starting with dreamer, moving to realistic and then to critic), first remember a time when you were either creative, realistic or critical. This will help you access that style and apply it to the current situation.
4069: In each frame of mind, address the issue at hand solely from that perspective. This will let you get the most out of each perspective, revealing more options and ideas.
4070: • In the dreamer space, let your ideas flow freely.• In the realist space, think about how the ideas you have created can be implemented. How can they be achieved? What needs to happen?• In the critic space, question and challenge your ideas and plan. Identify strengths and weaknesses; look for flaws; look for gaps or potential problems. Determine what needs to be done better.
4071: Once you have completed these four stages, go back to the beginning and re-evaluate your original dream and plan through each thinking stage in turn. You can repeat this process until you feel the plan works well from each perspective.
4072: Types of questions to ask at each stage
4073: Dreamer• Why am I doing this?• Can it be done better?• What would I like to happen?• Wouldn’t it be great if…..?• What reward or result would I like?
4074: Realist• How can I make that happen?• Who else do I need to make it work?• What needs to happen – and when?• What resources do I need?• How much will it cost?
4075: CriticDoes the idea really have potential?Is the objective achievable?Are there any barriers or resource issues?Does the plan work? Consider issues such as timing, cost or market potential.How can the plan be improved – are there gaps or are some things unnecessary?

4077: THE MATE MODEL FOR STRATEGIC SELLINGAchieving your sales objectives
4078: Segmenting and managing your contacts within a client organization in terms of their support for your sales objectives is a highly effective way of developing client relationships and selling.
4079: Four steps
4080: • Step 1: define your unique sales objective.Be clear about what you are selling and when, and the value it brings. What makes it an attractive proposition? What is its value for the organization or client? This sounds simple but it can be muddled or overlooked, with disastrous consequences.
4081: • Step 2: identify all the players using the MATE model.MATE highlights the need to focus on Money, Allies, Technical experts and End users. Identify each contact (including those you don’t know), recording their job title and name.
4082: MoneyThey have the ultimate veto on sales
4083: AlliesThey provide useful information, can guide you and influence others to support your objective
4084: Technical experts/assessorsThey filter out information, can be gatekeepers, can influence ‘Money’
4085: End usersThey use, manage or work with your products
4086: • Money. The budget holder has authority over the decision to spend. They tend to focus on the bottom line and have the power of veto. They will ask: ‘What impact will this have and what return will we get?’• Allies /Advocates. These can help guide you during the sales process. They provide valuable information, can lead you to the right people and may be influential. Allies are both inside and outside the organization.• Technical experts. They are gatekeepers who evaluate technical aspects of the proposal. They do not have final approval but offer recommendations to the decision-maker. They can say ‘no’ on account of technical issues. They ask whether the product or service matches their specifications.• End users. They judge the impact of your proposal on their job performance. They will implement or work with your solution, so their success is linked to your product and they will want to influence the decision to buy. They ask: Will it work for me or my department?
4087: • Step 3: consider each individual’s level of support.Having placed each individual on the MATE model, assess their level of support for your sales objective as high, medium or low.
4088: • Step 4: consider each individual’s level of influence.Assess each individual’s influence within their organization — high, medium or low.
4089: Check for warning signs
4090: Ensure that there are no threats to the sale by asking yourself the following:• Have I at least one person for each area?• Am I free from concerns about their influence?• Have I made personal contact with them?• Do I know their response modes and what they are looking for?
